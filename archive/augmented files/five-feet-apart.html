The Story in Brief

Seventeen-year-old Stella Grant has spent most of her life in and out of hospitals managing cystic fibrosis. She's a model patient — obsessive about her medication schedule, her treatment regimen, her digital sketchbook documenting life at Saint Grace's Hospital. Then Will Newman arrives for a drug trial to treat B. cepacia, a bacterial infection that would make Stella ineligible for the lung transplant she desperately needs. CF patients must stay six feet apart to avoid cross-infection. Stella and Will, drawn to each other despite the danger, decide they can steal back one foot. They'll stay five feet apart instead.

Rachael Lippincott's debut novel — expanded from a screenplay by Mikki Daughtry and Tobias Iaconis — became a surprise hit in 2018, part of the wave of illness-centered YA romances following The Fault in Our Stars. Director Justin Baldoni's 2019 film adaptation, starring Haley Lu Richardson as Stella and Cole Sprouse as Will, brought the story to a wider audience but divided critics who questioned whether it romanticized terminal illness or offered a genuine portrait of young people living with mortality. The film earned over $91 million worldwide, cementing its place as a teen tearjerker phenomenon even as medical consultants debated its portrayal of cross-contamination protocols.

Key Differences

Characterization

Lippincott's novel alternates between Stella's and Will's perspectives, giving each teenager a distinct interior life. Stella's chapters reveal her as a control addict who uses her treatment app and color-coded medication schedule to impose order on a body that's betraying her. When she realizes she's missed a dose because she was with Will, the panic isn't just medical — it's existential. Will's chapters show him as someone who's already given up: he's stopped taking his medications, stopped doing his treatments, stopped pretending he'll live long enough for any of it to matter. His sarcasm isn't quirky teen rebellion; it's a defense mechanism against hope.

Richardson and Sprouse deliver committed performances, but the film flattens these arcs into recognizable types: the Type-A girl who needs to loosen up, the bad boy who needs saving. Will's medication non-compliance becomes a plot point the film resolves too easily — one pep talk from Stella and he's back on track. The book shows his self-destruction as a months-long pattern that predates meeting Stella, rooted in watching his older brother die of the same disease. The film cuts the brother's death entirely, eliminating the foundation of Will's fatalism. Stella's best friend Poe — a gay teen with CF who's estranged from his homophobic parents — gets his own chapters in the book, especially a wrenching sequence where his boyfriend breaks up with him because watching Poe deteriorate is too painful. The film reduces Poe to comic relief and a narrative device, his boyfriend appearing only in a single reconciliation scene that earns none of its emotion.

Emotional Impact

The book's biggest emotional beats are quiet and internal. When Stella learns her friend Camila has died waiting for a transplant, Lippincott gives her two pages of silence — Stella sitting in the hospital chapel, not praying, just staring at the stained glass and thinking about all the hours she and Camila spent planning what they'd do after their transplants. The film shows Stella crying in Will's arms, turning Camila's death into fuel for the central romance rather than a meditation on survivor's guilt.

Will's decision to leave Stella comes from different motivations in each version. In the book, he learns his bacterial infection could kill her if they keep breaking the six-foot rule — and he realizes he's been selfish enough to risk her life for his own happiness. He leaves without explanation because he knows Stella would argue, would calculate risks, would convince herself she can manage it. The film has Will leave because he doesn't want Stella to watch him die, a decision that's more cinematically dramatic but less morally complex. The book makes Will's departure an act of love that requires him to become the villain in Stella's story. The film makes it a noble sacrifice that the narrative affirms.

The pool scene — where Stella falls through ice and nearly drowns — plays as a metaphor in the book. Stella has spent the novel carefully calculating risks, staying exactly five feet from Will, and the ice break represents the universe's indifference to her caution. She could die doing everything right. The film shoots it as a rescue sequence, Will pulling Stella from the water in slow motion, their kiss inevitable. One version uses the scene to destabilize Stella's worldview; the other uses it to bring the leads together.

Pacing and Structure

Lippincott structures the novel in short, present-tense chapters that create the rhythm of hospital time — the medications every four hours, the chest physiotherapy sessions, the moments of waiting that stretch and compress. An entire chapter covers the eight minutes of Stella's nebulizer treatment, her thoughts spiraling from what she'll say to Will next to whether she'll live to see twenty. This structure makes the reader feel the tedium and hypervigilance of chronic illness. The film adopts a conventional three-act romance structure: meet-cute in the hospital corridor, montage of stolen moments, third-act separation and reconciliation.

The book spends five chapters on Stella's decision to break the six-foot rule. She researches cross-contamination risks, calculates the statistical probability of infection, makes a pros-and-cons list, talks to her doctor about the data. By the time she tells Will they can stand five feet apart instead of six, we understand exactly what she's risking and why. The film compresses this into a single scene — Stella measuring five feet with her pool cue, grinning at Will. It's visually clever but erases the ethical weight of the decision. The book treats the one-foot transgression as seriously as the characters do; the film treats it as a meet-cute device.

The novel dedicates substantial space to medical procedures — PICC line insertions, sputum cultures, arterial blood gases. These aren't just atmospheric details; they're the texture of Stella's and Will's lives. When Will finally agrees to restart his treatments, Lippincott shows him doing chest PT, coughing up mucus, dealing with the physical misery he'd been avoiding. The film montages his recovery in thirty seconds of him doing breathing exercises while Stella watches encouragingly through glass. The book's pacing makes illness an ongoing reality; the film's pacing treats it as an obstacle to romance.

Thematic Depth

The novel interrogates what it means to live when you know you're dying young. Stella's obsessive rule-following isn't presented as something she needs to overcome — it's what's kept her alive longer than most CF patients. When she breaks her own rules for Will, the book doesn't celebrate her newfound spontaneity. It examines whether love justifies risking the transplant she's been working toward for years. Stella's final decision to pursue a transplant even though it means she and Will can never safely be together isn't framed as tragic; it's framed as her choosing her own survival over romance, which the novel treats as valid.

The book also explores the ethics of how illness is perceived. Stella maintains a YouTube channel about living with CF, posting cheerful videos about her treatments and hospital stays. Late in the novel, she admits these videos are propaganda — she's performing optimism because people don't want to see a sick teenager who's angry or scared. They want inspiration. Will calls her out on this, and Stella eventually posts a raw, unedited video about how exhausting it is to be everyone's inspiration instead of just a person with a disease. The film includes Stella's YouTube channel but only as a quirky character trait. She never grapples with the performance of illness or the pressure to be an acceptable sick person.

Will's refusal to comply with treatment becomes a question about autonomy. His doctors, his parents, and eventually Stella all insist he fight to stay alive. Will argues that it's his body and his choice — if he wants to spend his final months doing things he enjoys instead of sitting in a hospital, that's his right. The book lets this argument breathe, showing both sides. The film resolves it by having Will accept treatment, implying he was wrong to resist. The book's Will doesn't capitulate; he agrees to try treatment as an experiment, but he never fully abandons his belief that refusing aggressive medical intervention is a legitimate choice.

Ending

The book ends with Stella getting her transplant. Will has left town for treatment elsewhere, and they've agreed not to contact each other — Stella's new lungs will make her even more vulnerable to his bacteria. The final chapter is Stella's first day with healthy lungs, walking outside without oxygen, breathing cold air without coughing. She thinks about Will and acknowledges she'll probably always love him, but she's chosen herself. There's no reunion, no promise of future contact. The novel ends on Stella alone, alive, and okay with that.

The film ends with Stella getting her transplant and a time-jump reunion with Will. They meet outside the hospital, stay six feet apart, and exchange meaningful looks that suggest they'll find a way to be together despite the medical risks. It's framed as hopeful and romantic. Where the book ends with Stella accepting that love sometimes means permanent separation, the film suggests that true love transcends physical barriers — a fundamentally different message. The book's ending respects the real-world consequences of CF cross-contamination; the film's ending prioritizes romantic closure over medical reality. Lippincott leaves readers with an image of survival that doesn't require a love interest. Baldoni leaves viewers with the promise that these teenagers might still get their fairy tale, which undercuts the entire premise of why five feet mattered in the first place.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first — not because the film is poorly made, but because watching the film will make you think you already know this story, and you won't. Baldoni's adaptation translates the premise but not the substance. If you watch Cole Sprouse and Haley Lu Richardson first, you'll see a well-executed teen romance about overcoming obstacles. Then you'll read Lippincott's novel and realize the obstacles weren't meant to be overcome — they were meant to force impossible choices about how much of yourself you'll sacrifice for another person. The book is about two people who love each other and still can't be together, and the film retrofits that into a story about two people who find a way.

Going the other direction works against you. If you read the book first, the film will feel like it's sanded down every edge that made the story uncomfortable — but you'll at least understand what's missing and why those edges mattered. If you watch first, you'll get a perfectly competent weepie that hits its marks, and you might not bother with the book at all. That's the real loss. Lippincott's novel uses the romance framework to examine what chronically ill teenagers lose beyond their health — control, privacy, the luxury of thinking they'll have time to fix their mistakes. The film uses chronic illness as a backdrop for romance. Start with the version that's about something.

Verdict

The book lets its teenagers choose survival over romance and treats that as a legitimate ending. The film can't resist a reunion, even if it means ignoring the medical realities that structured the entire story. Lippincott writes about illness; Baldoni films around it. If you want the love story, watch the movie. If you want the story about living with chronic illness that happens to include love, read the book.
