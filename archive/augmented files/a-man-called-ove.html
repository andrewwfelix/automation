The Story in Brief

Fredrik Backman's 2012 novel follows fifty-nine-year-old Ove, a widower who enforces his Swedish neighborhood's parking rules with dictatorial precision and contemplates suicide after losing his wife Sonja. His plans are interrupted when the pregnant Iranian immigrant Parvaneh backs a trailer into his mailbox, beginning an unlikely friendship that draws Ove back into life. The novel alternates between present-day encounters — Ove teaching Parvaneh to drive his Saab, rescuing a fallen man from the train tracks, taking in a freezing cat — and flashbacks revealing his courtship with Sonja, the miscarriage that cost them children, and the bureaucratic fight over her teaching job that defined his hatred of officialdom.

Hannes Holm's 2015 Swedish adaptation casts Rolf Lassgård as the older Ove and Filip Berg as his younger self, compressing the novel's sprawling timeline into a tighter 116 minutes. The film keeps Backman's structure of intercut timelines but speeds through Ove's suicide attempts to reach the community-building scenes more quickly. It softens some of the novel's rage — Ove's feuds with his neighbor Rune and the condominium board — while emphasizing the physical comedy of his interactions with Parvaneh's bumbling husband Patrick. The film earned two Oscar nominations for Best Foreign Language Film and Best Makeup, became Sweden's highest-grossing film of 2015, and sparked a 2022 American remake with Tom Hanks that no one asked for.

Key Differences

Ove's Backstory

The novel dedicates entire chapters to Ove's childhood with his silent, principled father — the scene where they build a house together after his mother's death, the moment his father dies under a train Ove was supposed to inspect, the years Ove spent working railway maintenance while saving for technical school. These passages establish that Ove's rigidity comes from his father's working-class code: you do the job right, you keep your word, you don't complain. The film condenses all this into brief flashbacks that show his father's death and his meeting with Sonja on the train but skip the formative details. We never see young Ove building that house or learn that his obsession with proper tools and techniques is inherited grief made concrete.

Backman also explains Ove's Saab loyalty through a thirty-page sequence where Ove's father saves for years to buy a Saab 92, only to die before driving it, leaving Ove to inherit both the car and the brand allegiance. The film reduces this to a single line about Saab being his father's car. More crucially, the novel includes the episode where teenage Ove, newly orphaned, gets swindled out of his house by a fast-talking real estate agent — an experience that cements his distrust of white-collar professions and flowery language. The film omits this entirely, which leaves Ove's contempt for the "white shirts" from the condo association feeling like general curmudgeon behavior rather than a trauma response. Without these chapters, Lassgård plays Ove as grumpy by nature; in the book, he's grumpy by accumulation.

Ove's Relationships

Backman gives Parvaneh a sharp, specific voice — she calls Ove "the neighbor who is madder than everyone else" and teases him about his "make-everything-a-battle" attitude while also recognizing his loneliness. The novel devotes scenes to her bringing him food, asking him to fix her radiator, and demanding he babysit her daughters so she can take a driving lesson. Each interaction builds her understanding that Ove needs to be needed. She doesn't fix him through kindness; she conscripts him into usefulness. The film, with Bahar Pars in the role, keeps the driving lessons and the borrowed tools but rushes through the emotional progression. Pars plays Parvaneh as generically warm rather than strategically persistent, and the script gives her fewer verbal sparring matches with Ove. The result feels like a standard "foreigner teaches grump to love again" narrative instead of a comedy of mutual exploitation.

The novel also gives substantial weight to Ove's friendship with Rune, his neighbor and former best friend, now suffering from Alzheimer's and facing forced institutionalization by his wife Anita. Backman includes flashbacks to Ove and Rune's Saab-versus-Volvo rivalry — a decades-long comic debate over automotive superiority that masks genuine affection. When Ove helps Anita hide Rune from the social services agents trying to take him away, it's the climax of a forty-year relationship. The film simplifies Rune to a background figure. We see him in a wheelchair and hear that he and Ove had a falling-out, but the Saab-Volvo jokes are reduced to a single exchange, and the flashbacks to their friendship last under two minutes total. The film's Ove helps Anita as an abstract good deed, not as an act of reconciliation with the only man who understood him besides Sonja.

Tone and Pacing

Backman punctuates Ove's suicide attempts — hanging, carbon monoxide poisoning, walking onto the tracks — with dry comic narration that underscores the absurdity without mocking his despair. When Parvaneh interrupts his first attempt by asking him to help her husband back up the trailer, the novel notes that "Ove had never seen a worse reverse-parking maneuver in his life. It was as if the man was steering with his feet." The humor coexists with the real fact that Ove spent the previous chapter writing letters about his funeral arrangements. The film plays these scenes as slapstick: Ove is literally tying the noose when Parvaneh knocks, he puts on a shirt to answer the door, then returns to the noose still wearing the shirt — which makes the audience laugh but erases the novel's tension between Ove's genuine wish to die and his ingrained instinct to be helpful.

The novel also lingers on scenes the film treats as transitional. When Ove finds the stray cat bleeding on his porch, Backman spends pages on Ove's internal debate about whether to help it, his irritation when the cat won't leave, and his eventual trip to buy cat food while muttering that he doesn't even like cats. The repetition of "Ove doesn't like cats" becomes a refrain that measures his softening. The film shows the cat, shows Ove reluctantly petting it, and moves on. Holm's pacing prioritizes forward momentum — the 116-minute runtime zips through subplots the novel spends chapters on — which makes the film feel lighter and more heartwarming but less emotionally complex. You leave the theater smiling; you leave the book remembering what it feels like to want to die and to choose, grudgingly, to live anyway.

Emotional Impact

The novel's flashbacks to Sonja are specific about what Ove lost. We see her convince him to travel despite his hatred of airports, watch her spend hours reading to special-needs students after her accident left her unable to have children, and witness the bureaucratic battle where school administrators tried to force her into early retirement because her wheelchair was "disruptive." Backman shows that Sonja wasn't a symbol of warmth who softened Ove's edges — she was a woman who fought for everything and refused to let Ove retreat into bitterness. The chapter where she dies doesn't show her death; it shows Ove dismantling the wheelchair ramp he built for her, unable to throw away the bolts. The grief is in the details: Ove still sets the table for two, still expects to hear her voice correcting his grammar.

The film casts Ida Engvoll as young Sonja and gives her perhaps ten minutes of screen time total. We see her smile at Ove on the train, see her in a hospital bed after the bus accident that paralyzed her, and see her in a coffin. She's lovely and warm and underwritten. The film tells us Ove loved her but doesn't build the case for why she, specifically, mattered — what arguments they had, what jokes they shared, what she did that no one else would have done. When Lassgård's Ove visits her grave and kneels in the snow, it's touching because Lassgård sells the emotion, not because the script has earned it. Backman earns it by showing us six years of miscarriages, the travel montage where Sonja dragged Ove across Europe, and the scene where she made him promise not to become a bitter old man after she died — a promise the novel makes clear he's broken and is now, slowly, trying to keep.

Ending

The novel ends with Ove dying of heart failure in his sleep after successfully fighting off the condo board's attempt to institutionalize Rune — a death Backman presents as neither tragic nor triumphant but simply right. Ove gets a neighborhood funeral where Parvaneh's daughters plant flowers and the cat sits on his casket, and the final chapter reveals that Parvaneh's youngest daughter, born after Ove's death, is named after Sonja. The epilogue shows Parvaneh's husband Patrick taking over Ove's role as the neighborhood enforcer of parking rules, yelling at people in broken Swedish exactly the way Ove did. The implication is clear: Ove's legacy isn't that he became less rigid, but that he found people worth being rigid for. The book closes on the image of the Saab still parked in Ove's spot, which Parvaneh refuses to let anyone tow.

The film keeps Ove's death but stages it as a peaceful conclusion after a montage of him playing with Parvaneh's daughters and smiling — a visual argument that he died happy. Holm includes the funeral and the baby named Sonja but cuts the epilogue with Patrick. More significantly, the film ends on a flashback to young Ove and Sonja dancing on the train, a piece of original sentimentality Backman never wrote. It's a clean emotional note that reassures the audience Ove is reunited with his wife, which is comforting and tidy and betrays the novel's refusal to promise anything beyond death. Backman's ending is about what Ove left behind in the world; Holm's is about Ove leaving the world. One is a story about community; the other is a story about closure.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first — the film assumes you'll bring emotional context the script doesn't provide. If you watch Lassgård yell at someone for not sorting recycling correctly without having read the chapters explaining that Ove's father taught him "if you do something, do it right," the movie plays as a quirky comedy about a grump softening. With the book's backstory, the same scene is about a man whose entire moral framework is built on the idea that small acts of carelessness are how the world falls apart. The film is pleasant but thin; the novel gives you the weight the film's performances are trying to carry.

Watching first isn't disastrous — Holm's adaptation is faithful enough that you won't be confused by the book's additions, and Lassgård's performance gives you a clear image to hold onto when reading. But you'll spend the novel realizing how much the film glossed over, particularly in the Rune subplot and the Sonja flashbacks, and wishing you'd encountered Backman's version of these relationships first. The film works as a reminder of the book after you've read it; as an introduction, it undersells what Backman actually accomplished.

Verdict

The book gives you Ove's entire life — the father who taught him to build things, the wife who taught him to feel things, the neighbor who taught him that loyalty matters more than being right. The film gives you Rolf Lassgård scowling in a cardigan for two hours, which is charming but not the same. Read Backman's novel for the version that trusts you to sit with grief; watch Holm's film when you want to remember why you liked the book.
