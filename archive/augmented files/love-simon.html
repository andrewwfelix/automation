The Story in Brief

Sixteen-year-old Simon Spier lives in suburban Atlanta with his therapist mother Emily, his cooking-obsessed father Jack, and his younger sister Nora. He's got three best friends—Nick, Leah, and Abby—and a comfortable life, except for one secret: he's gay, and no one knows. Simon exchanges emails with an anonymous classmate who signs his messages "Blue," another closeted student at Creekwood High. When class clown Martin Addison discovers printouts of these emails, he blackmails Simon into helping him date Abby, threatening to out both Simon and Blue if Simon refuses. What follows is Simon's messy, anxious navigation of his identity, his friendships, and his attempt to figure out which of his classmates is Blue—possibilities include the sensitive Cal, orchestra member Bram, and soccer player Garrett.

Greg Berlanti's 2018 adaptation, retitled Love, Simon, became the first major studio teen rom-com centered on a gay protagonist. Fox marketed it broadly rather than as niche LGBTQ+ fare, and it earned $66 million worldwide—modest by blockbuster standards but significant for the genre. Nick Robinson plays Simon with an earnest, anxious energy, while Jennifer Garner and Josh Duhamel take on his parents. The film softens Albertalli's sharper edges, heightening the feel-good elements and sanding down moments of genuine discomfort. It became a cultural touchstone for young queer audiences who'd never seen themselves in a mainstream studio romance, even as critics noted its safer, more palatable approach compared to the novel's messier emotional terrain.

Key Differences

Narrative Perspective

Albertalli writes Simon in close first person—every chapter is his voice, his observations, his internal monologue spiraling through anxiety and hope. When Martin corners him in the library, we're inside Simon's head as panic sets in: "My heart is racing and my face feels hot and I want to throw up." The prose captures the specific cadence of a teenager overthinking every interaction, rewinding conversations, imagining worst-case scenarios. We hear Simon's unfiltered thoughts about his friends, his frustrations with Leah's clinginess, his guilt over manipulating Nick and Abby's budding relationship to satisfy Martin's blackmail demands. The book's intimacy comes from this unmediated access—Simon isn't performing for anyone, and Albertalli lets him be petty, self-centered, and deeply sympathetic all at once.

The film shifts to third-person omniscience, and while it includes voice-over narration from Robinson, the effect is explanatory rather than revelatory. Simon tells us what's happening—"I had a perfectly normal life"—but the narration functions as a framing device, not as the texture of the story itself. Berlanti's camera watches Simon from the outside, and Robinson's performance, warm and appealing as it is, can't replicate the book's claustrophobic interiority. The film also gives screen time to Martin's perspective, to Simon's parents' reaction, even to brief cutaways of Blue's life—choices that democratize the storytelling but flatten Simon's specificity. What the book achieves through voice, the film tries to achieve through music cues and reaction shots, and the trade-off is real: we like Simon, but we don't live in his head.

Character Development

In Albertalli's novel, Simon's friend group has distinct, complicating details. Leah plays guitar, quotes obscure folk singers, and harbors an unspoken crush on Simon that makes her increasingly jealous of Abby, the new girl whose confidence and ease threaten Leah's position in the group. Nick is a gentle, goofy kid who loves soccer and terrible puns, and his slow-building relationship with Abby is sweet and awkward in equal measure. Abby herself is dealing with the aftermath of leaving her old school and a bad situation back in D.C., something she reveals to Simon in a moment of real vulnerability. Simon's parents are fully realized, too: his mom is a therapist who sometimes psychoanalyzes him without meaning to, his dad makes elaborate brunches and cries at emotional moments, and his younger sister Nora is fiercely protective and wise beyond her years. These aren't archetypes—they're specific people with their own preoccupations.

The film compresses and simplifies. Leah, played by Katherine Langford, still pines for Simon, but the screenplay reduces her to a moody, jealous figure without giving her the musical passion or the lyrical, melancholy interiority the book provides. Nick, played by Jorge Lendeborg Jr., becomes the comic relief best friend whose relationship with Abby is cute but thinly sketched—his proposal to her via a school-wide announcement feels like a set piece rather than the culmination of a believable arc. Alexandra Shipp's Abby radiates charm but never gets the scene where she opens up about her past; she's the cool new girl and nothing more. Simon's parents, played by Garner and Duhamel, fare better—Garner's monologue about loving Simon exactly as he is became the film's emotional anchor—but Nora, played by Talitha Bateman, is mostly relegated to reaction shots at the dinner table. The film's focus on Simon leaves everyone else feeling like they're playing roles in his story rather than living their own.

Tone and Humor

Albertalli's humor is self-deprecating and specific, rooted in Simon's tendency to narrate his own life with a mix of irony and earnestness. He describes Martin as "the human embodiment of an unskippable YouTube ad." He imagines coming-out scenarios with absurd specificity: "I could hire a skywriter. That would be badass." When he's anxious, he catalogs small, irrelevant details—the pattern on his bedspread, the exact arrangement of his desk supplies—as a way of avoiding bigger feelings. The book's comedy comes from Simon's voice, which is hyper-literate without being pretentious, funny without trying too hard. It's the humor of a smart kid who uses language as a defense mechanism, and it never undercuts the emotional stakes. Even the novel's lighter scenes—Simon's dad attempting to give him a sex talk using a Harry Potter metaphor—are funny because they're grounded in character, not because they're playing for easy laughs.

The film's comedy is broader and more conventional. Berlanti includes slapstick moments like Simon crashing his car into a mailbox while distracted, or a musical fantasy sequence where Simon imagines himself in a Nutella-themed romantic montage. These bits are charming but generic—they could appear in any teen rom-com. The screenplay gives Martin, played by Logan Miller, several over-the-top scenes where he performs in the school musical or makes grand gestures to win Abby's attention; he's less a realistic antagonist and more a caricature of awkward desperation. The film also leans heavily on pop music to signal mood, using upbeat indie tracks to smooth over moments that the book leaves prickly and unresolved. Where Albertalli lets Simon sit in his discomfort, Berlanti tends to lighten the moment with a needle drop or a sight gag. The result is pleasanter but less memorable—the film wants you to feel good, while the book wants you to feel everything.

Emotional Impact

The book's most wrenching scenes come from Simon's isolation and his fear of losing control. After Martin outs him by posting his emails on the school gossip site, Simon describes the hallways of Creekwood as a gauntlet: students staring, whispering, some supportive but most just curious or uncomfortable. He stops going to lunch, hides in the library, and watches his friendships fracture—Leah won't speak to him because he knew about Nick and Abby and didn't tell her, Abby feels manipulated, and Nick is hurt that Simon didn't trust him. The betrayal Simon feels isn't just about Martin; it's about his agency being stolen, his story being told without his consent. Albertalli spends chapters in this painful space, showing Simon's shame, his anger, and his slow realization that he has to rebuild. The eventual reconciliation with his friends isn't a single conversation—it's a series of small, awkward interactions where trust gets pieced back together. When Simon finally meets Blue—who turns out to be Bram, the quiet kid from the school orchestra—the moment is understated, even a little anticlimactic, which makes it feel real.

The film condenses the fallout from Simon's outing into a montage of sad looks and avoidance, resolved within fifteen minutes of screen time. Robinson plays Simon's hurt effectively, but the script doesn't linger. The reconciliation happens quickly: Abby forgives him in a single scene, Leah comes around after a brief argument, and Nick was never really mad to begin with. The film adds a grand gesture—Simon climbs onto a Ferris wheel at a carnival and publicly declares his identity, asking Blue to meet him—that works as a cinematic climax but trades the book's quiet vulnerability for something more performative. When Bram, played by Keiynan Lonsdale, reveals himself and they kiss atop the Ferris wheel while the crowd cheers, it's a triumphant rom-com ending. It's designed to make you cry happy tears, and it does. But the book's ending, where Simon and Bram sit together at Waffle House, talking nervously and trying to figure out if they actually like each other in person, is awkward and uncertain and far more honest about what it means to begin something new.

Romantic Subplot

The central mystery of Albertalli's novel is Blue's identity, and she structures the book as a detective story layered over a romance. Simon and Blue's emails are intimate and revealing—they talk about their families, their fears, their favorite childhood memories—but Blue is careful never to include identifying details. Simon narrows down the suspects: it could be Cal Price, the shy, nerdy boy who seems too nervous to be hiding a secret; or Bram Greenfeld, who's friendly but hard to read; or even Garrett Laughlin, the soccer player who once complimented Simon's shirt. Albertalli plants clues and red herrings, and Simon's growing affection for Blue is tangled up with his anxiety about whether they'll actually connect in real life. The emails themselves are a huge part of the book's appeal—they're funny, vulnerable, and specific, full of inside jokes and references that build a relationship we believe in before we even know who Blue is. The reveal happens late, in the final chapters, and the suspense holds because the book has made us as invested in the answer as Simon is.

The film keeps the email exchanges but streamlines them into a handful of scenes, and the mystery is less central. Berlanti shows us Blue typing his responses, always keeping his face just out of frame or obscuring his identity with camera angles, but the script reveals Blue's identity—Bram—much earlier through visual cues and editing choices. Attentive viewers can figure it out by the midpoint, and the film doesn't really try to hide it; the focus shifts from "who is Blue?" to "how will Simon and Bram finally meet?" The emails lose their texture—Nick Robinson's voice-over reads them aloud, but we don't see them written out or savor the specific phrasing the way we do in the book. The film also invents a subplot where Simon tries to set up his friends with potential Blue candidates, leading to a series of awkward encounters played for comedy. It's an efficient way to keep the plot moving, but it replaces the book's slow-burn epistolary romance with something more mechanical. The connection between Simon and Bram in the film feels inevitable rather than earned, a romance delivered by the demands of the genre rather than discovered through correspondence.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first, especially if you care about Simon as an anxious, specific teenager rather than as a symbol of queer teen representation. Albertalli's novel lives in the small, uncomfortable moments—the times Simon snaps at his friends, the mornings he hides in the bathroom stall between classes, the nights he rereads Blue's emails and imagines a future he's not sure he's allowed to have. The film gives you Simon as a likable protagonist navigating a story with a predetermined happy ending. The book gives you Simon as a real person making mistakes, hurting people, and figuring out how to be honest when honesty feels impossible. If you watch the film first, you'll enjoy a warmhearted rom-com with genuine charm. If you read the book first, you'll understand what the film sanded off to make that charm possible, and you'll miss it.

Verdict

The book wins because Simon's voice—sarcastic, anxious, hyper-specific—is the story, and the film can't replicate it. Albertalli's novel lets Simon be messy and selfish and still deserving of love; Berlanti's film needs him to be sympathetic at all times. The book is a character study disguised as a rom-com. The film is a rom-com that happens to feature a well-drawn character.
