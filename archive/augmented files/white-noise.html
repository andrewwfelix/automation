The Story in Brief

Don DeLillo's White Noise follows Jack Gladney, a professor who founded the department of Hitler studies at the College-on-the-Hill, as he navigates death anxiety, a blended family of four children, and his marriage to Babette, who secretly takes an experimental drug called Dylar to suppress her own fear of dying. The novel's three sections—"Waves and Radiation," "The Airborne Toxic Event," and "Dylarama"—trace Jack's journey from the white noise of consumerist distraction through a chemical disaster that exposes him to Nyodene D., a carcinogen, and finally to his obsessive hunt for Willie Mink, the pharmaceutical researcher who slept with Babette in exchange for the drug. DeLillo structures the book as a series of fragmented conversations, supermarket observations, and media-saturated interior monologues that feel less like traditional scenes than philosophical jazz riffs on American dread.

Noah Baumbach's 2022 adaptation, starring Adam Driver as Jack and Greta Gerwig as Babette, was Netflix's first major attempt to crack what many considered an unfilmable postmodern novel. Baumbach keeps DeLillo's three-part structure and shoots the airborne toxic event as a darkly comic disaster spectacle complete with vibrant sunsets caused by chemical contamination. The film casts Don Cheadle as Murray Jay Siskind, Jack's colleague who delivers monologues about car crashes and Elvis with deadpan intensity, and André Benjamin as the Gladney family physician who refuses to tell Jack whether his exposure will kill him. It premiered at Venice and earned mixed reviews—critics praised Driver's neurotic energy and Baumbach's formal ambition but noted the film's struggle to translate DeLillo's prose rhythms into cinematic language. The movie became a litmus test for whether prestige streaming could handle high literary satire or whether some novels exist best on the page.

Key Differences

Tone and Style

DeLillo's novel moves through long stretches without plot—Jack and Murray debate the iconography of Hitler versus Elvis for pages, the Gladney children recite brand names and television dialogue in their sleep, and Jack spends an entire chapter wandering the supermarket as if it were a cathedral. The prose is coolly observational, almost anthropological, treating American consumer culture as an alien civilization. Sentences stack up in rhythmic, repetitive patterns: "The stereo stuff, the loud stuff, the soft stuff, the pink stuff, the baby stuff." DeLillo never signals when he's being satirical. He reports. The reader does the work of recognizing absurdity.

Baumbach shoots these moments as stylized tableaux—the supermarket becomes a brightly lit soundstage where extras glide through aisles in slow motion while LCD Soundsystem's "New Body Rotten" plays over the credits. It's visually arresting, but it announces its artifice. The dinner table conversations, which in the book drift through non sequiturs about death and cereal brands, become scripted exchanges that feel workshopped. Driver delivers Jack's interior anxiety through physical tics—hunched shoulders, nervous laughter—but the novel's white noise, that constant static hum of media and product names, becomes an aesthetic choice rather than a structural condition. Baumbach adds a satirical gloss where DeLillo embedded satire in syntax. The film knows it's funny. The book never blinks.

Character Development

Jack Gladney in the novel is a man whose entire identity is performance—he wears dark glasses and academic robes to teach, invented his middle initial, and built a career on a dictator whose language he doesn't speak. His fear of death manifests not in dramatic breakdowns but in obsessive small talk with Murray about which of them will die first and whether the survivors at a plane crash site are more afraid or less. Babette teaches posture to the elderly and compulsively chews gum, frozen dinners, and eventually Dylar. Her fear is quiet, domestic, revealed only gradually through Jack's observations and her confession late in the book. Their daughter Denise, suspicious and watchful, discovers Babette's pills and confronts her mother with the methodical coldness of a prosecutor. The children—Heinrich, Steffie, Denise, Wilder—are distinct skeptics and dreamers who speak in fragments Jack barely understands.

The film condenses these interiorities into actor mannerisms. Driver plays Jack as openly neurotic from the first scene, his death anxiety worn on his sleeve rather than buried under academic posturing. Gerwig's Babette is warmer, more overtly maternal, her fear of death signaled through teary confessions rather than the novel's slow accumulation of behavioral tics. Denise, played by Raffey Cassidy, gets her detective scenes but loses the book's sense that she's a miniature adult navigating her parents' childishness. Don Cheadle's Murray delivers his lines with theatrical precision—his speech comparing car crashes to religious ritual lands every beat—but the novel's Murray is stranger, more opaque, a man who might be profoundly insightful or completely full of it. Baumbach needs his actors to project emotion. DeLillo's characters withhold it, and the withholding is the point.

Themes and Subtext

The book treats consumerism not as a target for satire but as the texture of consciousness itself. Jack doesn't critique the supermarket—he finds spiritual comfort in it. "The large doors swung inward. I heard chanting. Money was changing hands." Shopping is liturgy. Brand names are mantras. The novel never steps outside this worldview to condemn it; it simply renders it with relentless fidelity. The airborne toxic event is less a plot device than a metaphor made literal—death stops being an abstraction and becomes a cloud you can see, measure, and run from. But even evacuation becomes a media event. Jack's exposure is recorded by SIMUVAC, a corporation rehearsing disaster response. "You are a perfect victim," they tell him. "We couldn't have planned it better." The book suggests that late capitalism has colonized even our deaths.

Baumbach's film makes these themes explicit. He shoots the supermarket with hyper-saturated colors and gliding cameras that scream "this is about consumerism." The toxic event becomes a disaster movie pastiche, complete with traffic jams, panicked crowds, and the beauty of contaminated sunsets—Baumbach even has Jack photograph them. It works as spectacle, but the irony is foregrounded. Murray's dialogue about the American death wish, delivered in long unbroken takes, becomes thesis statement rather than one man's eccentric theory. The film climaxes with Jack confronting Willie Mink in a motel room, and Baumbach stages it as a violent farce—Jack shoots Mink, Mink shoots Jack, and both men end up bleeding and laughing. The novel plays the scene as existential slapstick without release. Baumbach resolves it with an embrace of absurdity. DeLillo offers no resolution, only the supermarket in the final pages, still humming, still selling.

Ending and Resolution

DeLillo ends White Noise at the supermarket, where the checkout lanes have been rearranged and the customers are confused, upset, and "concerned." It's a one-page coda after the Willie Mink confrontation. Jack observes shoppers studying their generic products with suspicion, searching for meaning in packaging. The tabloids promise miracle cures and UFO sightings. "The terminals are equipped with holographic scanners, which decode the binary secret of every item, infallibly." The novel doesn't conclude—it loops back to the static it started with. Jack hasn't conquered death or saved Babette. He's just back in the cathedral of consumption, where everything is coded and nothing is certain. The final image is of people waiting, staring, still afraid.

Baumbach adds an entire musical number. The film's final sequence is a choreographed dance through the supermarket aisles set to LCD Soundsystem, with the cast and extras gliding, spinning, and lip-syncing while pushing carts. It's visually inventive and tonally bizarre—a postmodern music video bolted onto a literary adaptation. Baumbach has said he wanted to honor DeLillo's ending while giving the audience catharsis. The dance suggests that if we can't escape the supermarket, we might as well enjoy it, embrace the absurdity, move to the rhythm of consumption. It's optimistic in a way the novel never is. DeLillo's ending is resigned, observational, almost clinical. Baumbach's is a celebration, or at least a shrug set to music. Whether that's a betrayal or a valid interpretation depends on whether you think White Noise offers any way out of the noise. DeLillo doesn't. Baumbach tries to choreograph one.

Comedic Tone

The novel's humor is bone-dry and cumulative. Jack's son Heinrich debates whether it's raining—"It's going to rain tonight," Jack says. "It's raining now," Heinrich replies, then argues that rain they see now is actually falling in the past because of the time it takes for perception to reach the brain. These conversations spiral for pages without punchlines. Murray advises Jack to read about murder as if it's a form of self-help. Babette reads tabloid horoscopes to blind seniors at the nursing home with complete sincerity. The comedy comes from DeLillo's refusal to editorialize. He presents these moments as simple documentation of how people talk, and the effect is deeply, quietly absurd. The Gladney family is surrounded by low-level apocalypse and treats it like weather.

Baumbach mines these scenes for laughs but can't resist underlining them. Driver plays Jack with a stammering intensity that signals comic neurosis—the Hitler studies scenes become academic satire with rimshot timing. The novel's Hitler studies are presented without commentary; the film treats them as obviously ridiculous. When the Gladney family evacuates during the toxic event and gets caught in traffic, Baumbach stages it as screwball chaos with honking horns, shouting extras, and escalating panic. DeLillo describes the same scene in flat, procedural prose—people wait, argue, check their watches. The absurdity is in the bureaucracy, the way simulation (SIMUVAC) replaces reality. Baumbach's comedy is loud and gestural. DeLillo's is quiet and systemic. The film gets laughs. The novel gets under your skin.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first if you want to experience White Noise as a novel of accumulation—where meaning builds through repetition, digressions, and DeLillo's specific syntax. The film will make more sense if you've spent time in the novel's rhythms, understanding that scenes which feel inert or circular on screen are in fact the book's method. Watching first won't ruin the novel, but it'll prime you to expect a plot-driven narrative when DeLillo is more interested in texture and recursion. You'll read the supermarket scenes looking for the dance number instead of absorbing their slow, hypnotic dread.

If you watch first, Baumbach's stylization might make the book feel sparse or underwritten—you'll expect the toxic event to be a spectacle and find instead a few chapters of bureaucratic confusion and strange sunsets. The novel's ending will feel like a non-ending because the film taught you to expect choreography. But if you're unlikely to read DeLillo otherwise, the film is a competent entry point to his concerns, even if it translates his prose into a more digestible idiom. Driver and Gerwig make Jack and Babette warm and accessible in ways the novel doesn't prioritize. Just know you're getting an interpretation, not a transcription. The white noise in the book is everywhere, inescapable, constitutional. In the film, it's a soundtrack.

Verdict

The book sustains its deadpan satire for over three hundred pages without breaking, while the film announces its cleverness at every turn. DeLillo's sentences do what Baumbach's cameras can't—render white noise as prose rhythm rather than production design. The film is ambitious and occasionally inspired, but the novel is the original transmission.
