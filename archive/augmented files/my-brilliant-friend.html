The Story in Brief

Elena Greco — called Lenù by everyone in her 1950s Naples neighborhood — narrates the origin story of her decades-long friendship with Raffaella Cerullo, known as Lila. The girls grow up in a violent rione where Don Achille Carracci terrifies the residents, where fathers beat their children bloody, and where marriage offers the only escape for girls denied education. Lila is the fiercer, more brilliant of the two — she teaches herself Latin while working in her father's shoe-repair shop — but it's Elena who gets to continue school beyond elementary grades. The novel tracks them from age six to sixteen, ending with Lila's wedding to Stefano Carracci, the neighborhood grocer who represents wealth and a ticket out. Their competition, mutual obsession, and unequal opportunities form the core of Ferrante's narrative.

Saverio Costanzo adapted the first novel into a feature film in 2018, casting young unknowns Elisa Del Genio and Ludovica Nasti as the childhood versions of the friends, then Margherita Mazzucco and Gaia Girace as the teenage pair. The film preserves the major plot beats — the doll-throwing incident, the New Year's Eve fireworks battle, Elena's humiliation when she visits Ischia, Lila's disastrous wedding night — but condenses a 331-page psychological bildungsroman into two hours. Shot in Naples with non-professional child actors speaking in Neapolitan dialect, the film earned praise for its authenticity and visual severity. It arrived in theaters between the first and second seasons of the subsequent HBO series, confusing American audiences who had already encountered a more expansive adaptation on television.

Key Differences

Elena's Interior Monologue

Ferrante's novel is narrated by an elderly Elena looking back on her childhood, and the double perspective — the adult voice analyzing the child's feelings — creates a constant tension between what young Lenù understood and what the older Elena now sees. When Lila dismantles a novel Elena has written in middle school, tearing it apart paragraph by paragraph, Elena describes her devastation but also recognizes, decades later, that Lila was trying to teach her to write with more precision. The book is full of these retrospective clarifications: Elena admits she exaggerated her academic achievements to Lila, confesses she was jealous when Maestra Oliviero praised Lila's intelligence, and reveals she secretly hoped Lila would fail when Lila tried to teach herself Greek and Latin from Elena's textbooks.

Costanzo's film eliminates the older Elena entirely. We watch young Margherita Mazzucco's face in close-up as Lila critiques her writing, but we only guess at what she's feeling — envy, admiration, rage, shame. The film can't convey Elena's adult insight that Lila wasn't cruel but exacting, that she demanded more from Elena because she recognized Elena's potential. Without the double narration, the competitive intensity of their friendship becomes harder to parse. We see Elena studying late into the night, we see her defending Lila to Maestra Oliviero, but we don't hear her admit that every achievement she earned was performed primarily for Lila's attention. The film treats their bond as simpler than it is: loyal childhood friends separated by class and circumstance. Ferrante's Elena knows the friendship is more ambiguous — a site of love, jealousy, mutual exploitation, and something close to erotic obsession.

The Neighborhood as Character

In the novel, the rione is a claustrophobic labyrinth of vendettas, debts, and inherited hatreds. Ferrante dedicates pages to mapping the social hierarchy: Don Achille's wealth came from black-market dealings during the war; the Carracci family parlayed that into respectability through Stefano's grocery; the Solaras control the neighborhood through their bar and rumored camorra connections; Lila's father Fernando remains poor because he's an honest craftsman in a corrupt economy. Elena explains how her own father's limp — a war injury — grants the Greco family slight deference, but also marks them as vulnerable. She describes how Melina Cappuccio went mad after her affair with the poet Donato Sarratore ended, and how the neighborhood punished her visible suffering with contempt. Every family's trajectory is detailed across generations.

The film simplifies this genealogy drastically. Don Achille appears in one early scene — a shadowy figure in a doorway — and then he's murdered. We meet Stefano, the Solaras, Fernando, and Melina, but the intricate web of alliances and resentments that Ferrante charts over a hundred pages is reduced to visual shorthand: men arguing in the street, Melina throwing Sarratore family belongings out a window. Costanzo shoots the neighborhood in long, static takes — crumbling stairwells, narrow courtyards, laundry strung between buildings — that convey poverty and density but not the specific power dynamics. The novel makes clear that when Stefano courts Lila, he's attempting to buy his way into legitimacy by marrying the most beautiful girl in the rione, and that the Solaras see this as a betrayal because they want Lila for themselves. The film shows Stefano's courtship and the Solaras' anger, but without Ferrante's ethnographic detail, it plays as generic: rich men competing for a poor girl's attention.

Lila's Wedding and the Shoes

The Cerullo shoes are the novel's central symbol. Lila designs a pair of elegant men's shoes and convinces her father and brother to craft them, imagining the shoes as the foundation of a Cerullo footwear business that will lift the family out of poverty. When Stefano sees the shoes, he promises to finance a shoe factory if Lila marries him. Lila agrees — partly because she believes in her designs, partly to escape her father's violence. The betrayal comes at the wedding reception when Lila sees the shoes on Marcello Solara's feet. Stefano has sold the designs to the Solaras, the men Lila despises most. Ferrante describes Lila's realization in meticulous detail: "She looked at Marcello's feet, and on his feet she saw the shoes... the shoes that she and Rino had made, the shoes that were supposed to save the family, the shoes that Stefano had used to trick her into marriage. She felt something break inside her." The shoes represent everything — ambition, craft, autonomy, hope — and Stefano's betrayal destroys all of it.

Costanzo films the wedding reception with the same slow-building dread, and Gaia Girace's face when she notices the shoes is a study in dawning horror. But the film only glances at the shoe-design plot earlier. We see Lila sketching, we see Fernando dismissing her ideas, we see one brief scene where Stefano admires a prototype. The full weight of the betrayal — the months of labor Lila and Rino poured into the project, the specific humiliation of seeing her work on Marcello Solara's feet, the realization that Stefano viewed her as merchandise to be traded — doesn't land with the same devastation. The novel devotes fifty pages to the shoe project; the film gives it maybe five minutes. The wedding-night scene that follows is brutal in both versions, but in the book, we understand that Stefano's sexual violence is a continuation of his economic violence. In the film, it's a shocking finale that arrives without sufficient foundation.

Nino Sarratore and Elena's Romantic Awakening

Elena's infatuation with Nino Sarratore — the son of Donato, the poet who drove Melina mad — is one of the novel's sustained subplots. She first notices him in elementary school, fixates on him through middle school, and finally spends time with him during a summer vacation on Ischia when she's fifteen. Ferrante narrates Elena's obsession with painful precision: she describes how Elena rehearses conversations with Nino in her head, how she angles her beach chair to watch him swim, how she interprets every glance and half-smile as coded romantic communication. When Nino argues about politics and literature with his father, Elena is dazzled by his intellect. When he lends her books — Pavese, Vittorini — she stays up all night reading them so she can discuss them the next day. But the summer ends ambiguously: Nino seems interested in her, then doesn't write to her when she returns to Naples. Elena is left suspended in uncertainty.

The film includes the Ischia vacation but compresses Elena's obsession into a montage. Mazzucco gazes at the actor playing Nino, they have one conversation on the beach about poetry, and then the sequence ends. The novel's excruciating detail — the way teenage infatuation becomes a form of intellectual and erotic education for Elena — is absent. We don't see her jealousy when Nino pays attention to another girl, don't hear her internal debate about whether she's pretty enough, smart enough. The film treats the Ischia episode as a minor digression; the novel treats it as the moment when Elena begins to understand that intelligence and desire are linked, that Lila isn't the only person whose approval she craves. Nino becomes a major figure in subsequent novels in the quartet, but the film doesn't establish why he matters to Elena beyond a fleeting teenage crush.

Language and Class Dialect

Ferrante's novel is obsessed with language as a marker of class and intellect. Elena describes how she learned to speak in Italian at school while everyone in the neighborhood spoke Neapolitan dialect, how Maestra Oliviero corrected her grammar, how her parents' dialect embarrassed her when she visited wealthier classmates. Lila, despite being pulled from school after fifth grade, teaches herself to speak perfect Italian by reading voraciously — she borrows Elena's textbooks, reads novels from the lending library, and corrects Elena's pronunciation. When Lila writes a letter to Elena using formal Italian prose, Elena is both awed and humiliated: Lila has mastered in months what Elena struggled with for years. The novel makes clear that language is the primary tool of social mobility, and that Elena's education — her ability to speak, read, and write in standardized Italian — is the only advantage she has over Lila.

Costanzo made the admirable choice to shoot the film in Neapolitan dialect with Italian subtitles, rejecting the standard practice of having actors speak in standard Italian. This lends the film authenticity and grounds it in a specific regional culture. But it also eliminates the linguistic class tension that Ferrante foregrounds. In the book, when Elena speaks Italian at school and dialect at home, we see her code-switching as a survival strategy. In the film, everyone speaks the same language — dialect — so the hierarchy collapses. We lose the scenes where Elena corrects her mother's grammar, where Lila mocks the schoolteacher's pretentious vocabulary, where Elena practices pronunciation in front of a mirror. The film's commitment to dialect is a strength in terms of regional authenticity, but it erases one of the novel's central themes: that language is a battlefield, and that Elena's modest victory — escaping the neighborhood through education — depends on mastering the colonizer's tongue.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first — not because the film is incompetent, but because Ferrante's novel operates at a level of psychological granularity that no two-hour film can match. If you watch Costanzo's adaptation first, you'll see a well-acted period drama about two friends trapped by poverty and patriarchy. You'll miss the double narration that makes the novel a retrospective inquiry into how friendships shape identity, the way Elena's adult voice interrogates her childhood self's motives and jealousies. You'll miss the elaborate social mapping of the neighborhood, the way Ferrante turns a few city blocks into a complete world with its own economy, genealogy, and code of honor. Most crucially, you'll miss the slow accumulation of detail — the way Lila's brilliance is demonstrated not through one scene, but through dozens of small moments where she outthinks, out-reads, and out-maneuvers everyone around her.

The film works as a companion piece if you've already read the novel. You'll recognize the casting coups — Girace really does embody Lila's feral intensity — and appreciate Costanzo's refusal to prettify the neighborhood's violence. But watch it first and you'll wonder what the fuss is about. The friendship will seem interesting but not revelatory, the social commentary present but not cutting. Ferrante's genius is in the accretion of observed detail, the way she builds a complete psychology for Elena across hundreds of pages. The film gives you the plot. The book gives you the mechanisms of obsession, competition, and devotion that make the plot matter.

Verdict

The book wins by a wide margin. Ferrante's double narration and obsessive psychological detail can't be translated to film without losing what makes the novel essential — the sense that Elena is interrogating her own past, trying to understand how her friendship with Lila made and unmade her.
