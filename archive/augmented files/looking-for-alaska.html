The Story in Brief

Miles "Pudge" Halter leaves Florida for Culver Creek boarding school in Alabama seeking what the poet François Rabelais called "the Great Perhaps" — a last-words obsession that defines his character. He rooms with Chip "the Colonel" Martin and falls under the spell of Alaska Young, a self-destructive scholarship student who chain-smokes, quotes poetry, and keeps a tower of books in her dorm room. Alaska orchestrates pranks against the school's wealthy "Weekday Warriors," pulls Pudge into her orbit with late-night discussions about suffering and meaning, then dies in a car accident halfway through the story. The second half — labeled "After" to the first half's "Before" — follows Pudge and the Colonel as they obsess over whether Alaska's death was suicide or accident, ultimately realizing they'll never know.

Josh Schwartz and Stephanie Savage adapted the novel as a limited Hulu series in 2019, fourteen years after publication and four years after Green's The Fault in Our Stars became a box-office phenomenon. Kristine Froseth plays Alaska and Charlie Plummer plays Pudge across eight episodes. The adaptation arrived during a glut of YA streaming content and received polite but unenthusiastic reviews — critics praised the performances but noted the story's central tragedy hit harder on the page than on screen.

Key Differences

Characterisation of Alaska

Green's Alaska catalogues labyrinth imagery, quotes Rabelais and Gabriel García Márquez from memory, and admits to Pudge that she sabotaged her previous relationship because "I just get so scared of the future." She blames herself for her mother's death — she was eight years old, frozen in panic while her mother seized on the kitchen floor — and readers access this guilt through Pudge's limited understanding and Alaska's own drunken confession the night before she dies. The novel withholds clarity about whether she drove into the police cruiser on purpose or by accident, and that ambiguity becomes the thematic engine of the "After" section. Froseth's Alaska keeps the cigarettes and the obscure literary references, but the series gives her more screen time explaining herself. She tells Pudge directly about her mother's death in episode three, not episode seven. The moment loses the weight of revelation because it arrives before the audience has spent enough time wondering what drives her recklessness. The Hulu version also adds a scene in the final episode where the Colonel discovers a note in Alaska's copy of The General in His Labyrinth that implies she intended to visit her mother's grave the night she died — an invented detail that tilts the ambiguity toward accident and undermines the novel's insistence that Pudge will never know.

Depth of Exploration

Pudge's first-person narration in the novel is packed with footnotes about famous last words, tangents about his memorization process, and interiority about his lack of sexual experience and his desperate need to matter to someone. When Alaska kisses him during a game of Truth or Dare, he describes the physical sensations in excruciating detail, then spends two pages analyzing whether it meant anything. After her death, he writes an essay for his religion class about the nature of suffering, drawing on Buddhist and Christian frameworks to conclude that Alaska's final question — "How will I ever get out of this labyrinth?" — refers not to death but to life's suffering. The essay appears in full in the novel. The series shows Pudge writing the essay and Dr. Hyde (played by Ron Livingston) reading it aloud, but we hear only a few sentences. Without the full text, the viewer loses Pudge's intellectual framework for processing grief — the way he uses abstraction to avoid the messier emotional reckoning he can't articulate. Charlie Plummer's performance is restrained and credible, but the camera can't access the recursive self-consciousness that defines Pudge on the page. The series compensates with montages of Pudge and the Colonel driving around at night, set to indie rock, looking for answers. The montages look elegant but say nothing Pudge's voiceover couldn't have handled in fifteen seconds.

Pacing and Structure

Green splits the novel into two titled sections: "Before" and "After." Each chapter is labeled with a countdown or count-up — "one hundred thirty-six days before," "seven days after" — which anchors the reader in narrative time and builds dread toward the moment Alaska dies. The structure makes her absence visceral because the reader knows exactly how long they've been reading without her. The Hulu series preserves the countdown titles at the start of each episode, but streaming TV doesn't create the same dread. Viewers can see how many episodes remain. The series spends four episodes on "Before" and four on "After," which sounds symmetrical but creates a pacing problem: the novel devotes roughly two-thirds of its pages to "Before," letting Alaska dominate the narrative before she vanishes. The series splits the time evenly, so the investigation into her death feels protracted. Entire scenes are invented to fill space — a subplot about Takumi's vlog, a sequence where Pudge and the Colonel steal a school van to drive to the police station and request the accident report. None of this is in the book. The result is a second half that doesn't accelerate toward clarity but meanders toward resignation, which may be thematically appropriate but isn't dramatically satisfying.

Narrative Perspective

The novel's first-person narration means Pudge describes not only what happens but what he thinks is happening, and the gap between the two is where the characterization lives. When Alaska invites him to smoke with her in the woods, he interprets every gesture as potential romantic interest, then catches himself doing it and mocks his own desperation. The reader is always inside his head, watching him construct meaning from insufficient evidence. The Hulu series uses Pudge's voiceover occasionally — mostly to introduce the countdown — but long stretches of dialogue play without his commentary. This means Kristine Froseth has to convey Alaska's interiority through performance alone, without Pudge's misinterpretations guiding the viewer's understanding. Froseth is charismatic but the script doesn't give her enough contradictions to play. When Alaska tells Pudge "You're adorable" in episode two, it's flirtatious and clear. In the novel, the same line arrives buried in a paragraph where Pudge admits he doesn't know if she's mocking him or not. The series also adds scenes from the Colonel's perspective that Pudge never witnesses — a conversation with his mother about money, an argument with his girlfriend Sara. These scenes provide context the novel intentionally withholds, because Pudge is too self-absorbed to ask. The result is a more complete but less psychologically claustrophobic story.

Thematic Emphasis

Green structures the novel around Pudge's last-words collection and his religion class assignment to articulate a framework for hope in the face of suffering. The book returns repeatedly to Simón Bolívar's question — "How will I ever get out of this labyrinth of suffering?" — which Alaska has scrawled in the margins of her copy of The General in His Labyrinth. Pudge's final essay argues that the way out of the labyrinth is to forgive, both yourself and others, even when you don't have all the answers. The novel ends with Pudge saying "I forgive you, Alaska" aloud, even though he still doesn't know why she died. The series includes the forgiveness line but surrounds it with so much explanatory dialogue that the ambiguity dissolves. The final episode has Pudge tell the Colonel "Maybe she didn't want to die, maybe she just wanted to see her mom," which is a plausible interpretation but not one the book ever confirms. The show also cuts Dr. Hyde's lecture on Buddhist and Christian conceptions of suffering, replacing it with a single scene where he tells Pudge "You're asking the wrong question." The novel trusts the reader to sit with unanswered questions. The series, perhaps worried about viewer patience, offers answers the book refuses to provide.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first, because the Hulu series solves mysteries Green deliberately leaves open. If you watch Kristine Froseth play Alaska before you read her, you'll know what she looks like, how she moves, and — thanks to the invented graveyard note — what she was probably thinking the night she died. The book's power comes from not knowing, from living inside Pudge's desperate need for an answer he'll never get. The series provides closure. The novel provides something closer to grief.

The series works as a competent adaptation if you've already read the book and want to see Culver Creek visualized or hear Alaska's voice performed. But it can't reproduce the experience of reading Pudge's recursive, obsessive narration — the way he circles back to the same moments, reinterpreting them with new information, never landing on certainty. If you watch first, the book will feel repetitive. If you read first, the show will feel like a well-acted summary that mistakes explanation for understanding.

Verdict

The novel owns Pudge's interiority and trusts the reader to sit with ambiguity. The Hulu series offers strong performances from Plummer and Froseth but invents answers to questions Green refused to settle, diluting the thematic weight of not knowing.
