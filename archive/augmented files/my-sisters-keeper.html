The Story in Brief

Thirteen-year-old Anna Fitzgerald was conceived through preimplantation genetic diagnosis to be a perfect biological match for her older sister Kate, who was diagnosed with acute promyelocytic leukemia at age two. For eleven years, Anna has donated blood, bone marrow, and stem cells to keep Kate alive. Now Kate needs a kidney, and Anna hires lawyer Campbell Alexander to sue her parents — Sara, a former attorney who abandoned her career to manage Kate's illness, and Brian, a firefighter who retreats into silence — for medical emancipation. The lawsuit tears the family apart while eldest son Jesse, who's spent years ignored and acting out, watches from the margins. Picoult tells the story through rotating first-person chapters from Anna, Sara, Campbell, Brian, Jesse, and Kate's perspectives, plus third-person sections following Julia Romano, the guardian ad litem assigned to determine Anna's best interests and Campbell's ex-girlfriend from fifteen years prior.

Director Nick Cassavetes adapted the novel in 2009 with Cameron Diaz as Sara, Abigail Breslin as Anna, Sofia Vassilieva as Kate, and Alec Baldwin as Campbell. The film arrived five years after the book became a bestseller, riding the wave of young adult tear-jerker adaptations. Critics praised the performances — particularly Vassilieva, who shaved her head for the role — but faulted the screenplay for sentimentality and a radically altered ending that book readers found unforgivable. The film earned $95 million worldwide yet remains divisive among Picoult's readership, remembered more for what it changed than what it preserved.

Key Differences

The Ending's Reversal

Picoult's novel concludes with Anna winning medical emancipation, then dying in a car accident on the way home from court — Campbell survives the crash and honors her wishes by refusing the kidney donation, allowing Kate to die or survive on her own terms. The book's epilogue reveals Kate lived, became a dance teacher, and remains haunted by surviving at Anna's expense. The film inverts this entirely: Kate dies of her leukemia after Anna wins the case, and Anna lives to narrate Kate's funeral in voiceover. This isn't a minor tweak — it's a structural demolition. The book's power rests on the savage irony that Anna's autonomous choice becomes meaningless the moment she loses consciousness in the wreck, that Sara's worst fear comes true but in a way that renders her legal battle pointless. The film chooses the expected emotional beat — the sick sister dies, the family grieves — and abandons the moral vertigo that makes Picoult's version unbearable. Kate's death in the movie is sad; Anna's death in the book is philosophically devastating because it exposes how little control any of them ever had.

Campbell's Seizure Disorder and Service Dog

In the novel, Campbell has a service dog named Judge and claims he's trained for various ailments — cardiac issues, then psychiatric support — changing his story to avoid revealing he has epilepsy. His reluctance stems from losing Julia fifteen years earlier after a grand mal seizure; he disappeared rather than appear weak. The epilepsy becomes critical in the third act when Campbell has a seizure while driving, causing the accident that kills Anna. Julia's role as guardian ad litem forces them to confront their past, and the dog is a constant visual reminder of Campbell's hidden vulnerability. The film reduces Judge to a sight gag — Campbell has the dog but offers no explanation, and the epilepsy vanishes entirely. The car crash happens offscreen and without a medical cause, making it feel like screenwriter Jeremy Leven simply needed to remove a character. By cutting Campbell's condition, the movie eliminates the book's suggestion that his disability directly caused Anna's death, a connection that adds layers of guilt and inevitability. Judge becomes a quirky prop instead of a plot device, and Campbell shifts from a man concealing a condition he's ashamed of to a generically charming lawyer with an unexplained golden retriever.

Multiple Narrators vs. Linear Focus

Picoult gives chapters to Anna, Sara, Campbell, Brian, Jesse, and Kate herself — six voices, each with distinct syntax and obsessions. Sara's chapters read like legal briefs, cataloging symptoms and treatments with clinical precision. Brian's are quieter, filled with firefighting metaphors and suppressed grief. Jesse's seethe with resentment at being invisible while Kate monopolizes the family's emotional resources. Anna's voice is wry, detached, desperate for attention but guilty for wanting it. Campbell's are defensive, intellectualizing, afraid of intimacy. Kate's single chapter near the end reveals she asked Anna to refuse the kidney because she's exhausted and ready to die — the lawsuit was orchestrated by Kate herself, recontextualizing everything Anna has claimed. The film collapses this into a straightforward third-person narrative focused on Anna and Sara's conflict, with brief voiceover from Anna. Kate never narrates, so her deathbed confession that she wanted to die arrives as dialogue, not as the revelatory interior monologue Picoult constructs. Jesse nearly vanishes — his arson subplot, his rage at being the genetically useless son, his secret visits to Kate's hospital room — all cut. The polyphonic structure makes the book feel like a legal case built from contradictory testimonies; the film feels like a Cameron Diaz domestic drama with sick-kid trappings.

The Custody Hearing's Testimony

The book devotes over a hundred pages to the hearing, where Anna, Sara, Brian, and a medical expert testify. Sara argues on the stand that Anna is too young to understand the consequences of refusing the kidney donation, that the family functions as a unit and Anna's role is to save Kate. Brian admits he's conflicted but defers to Sara. Anna describes a lifetime of medical procedures she never consented to — the bone marrow extraction at age five that left her unable to walk for a week, the lymphocyte donations, the expectation that her body exists for Kate's use. The expert witness confirms that donating a kidney carries real risks, including possible kidney failure later in Anna's life. Picoult stages the hearing as a genuine ethical debate: parental rights, bodily autonomy, and the question of whether a child conceived as a donor has the same moral status as a child conceived for her own sake. The film compresses the hearing into a montage with snippets of testimony, no cross-examination, and a rushed verdict. Cameron Diaz gets one courtroom speech where she insists she loves both daughters equally, and Abigail Breslin gets a tearful moment, but there's no sustained interrogation of the ethics. The legal framework that makes the book feel urgent — the precedents Campbell cites, the constitutional questions Julia weighs — becomes background noise.

Jesse's Arson and Invisibility

In Picoult's version, Jesse is a seventeen-year-old arsonist who sets warehouse fires and drinks himself numb because he's been functionally orphaned by Kate's illness. Brian, a career firefighter, suspects Jesse is responsible but can't bring himself to confirm it because acknowledging Jesse's cry for help would mean admitting he's failed him. Jesse's chapters reveal that he tested his own blood to see if he was a match for Kate — he wasn't, and the knowledge that he's genetically useless to his family has destroyed him. Late in the book, Jesse confesses the fires to Brian, who holds him while he sobs. The film reduces Jesse to a background figure played by Evan Ellingson — he appears in maybe five scenes, says little, and his arson habit is mentioned once in passing. There's no scene where he confronts his parents about being ignored, no moment where Brian chooses between his role as father and his role as fire investigator. Jesse's invisibility is the point in the novel: he's the son who doesn't matter because he can't save Kate. The movie makes him literally invisible by refusing to develop him, but it's not a deliberate thematic choice — it's a narrative casualty of a two-hour runtime that prioritizes tearful mother-daughter scenes over the brother who's burning down buildings to feel noticed.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first — the film's ending changes the entire argument Picoult is making. If you watch the movie first, you'll expect a story about a sick girl who dies and a family that learns to let go. The book is about a healthy girl who dies in a random accident immediately after asserting her autonomy, rendering her legal victory and her family's suffering meaningless. That tonal difference is irreconcilable. The novel's final pages, narrated by Kate years later, describe living with survivor's guilt, dancing in a studio while Anna's absence follows her like a shadow. The film ends with Anna describing Kate's life from beyond the grave, a maudlin reversal that turns Picoult's existential gut-punch into a Hallmark card. If you read first, the film will frustrate you but won't overwrite the book's ideas. If you watch first, you'll think the book's ending is needlessly cruel when it's actually the sharper, braver choice.

Beyond the ending, the book's rotating perspectives give you access to Jesse's rage, Brian's helplessness, and Campbell's epilepsy-driven guilt — none of which survive adaptation. The courtroom scenes in the novel build a legal and ethical framework that makes Anna's case feel winnable and tragic; the film reduces the hearing to montage. Read first so you experience the version where every character gets to make their case, where the moral questions don't have easy answers, and where the ending doesn't reassure you that everything happens for a reason. The movie offers catharsis. The book offers something harder and more honest.

Verdict

The book is a structurally ambitious ethical inquiry built from six voices and a savage final twist; the film is a well-acted weepie that inverts the ending and discards the moral vertigo that justifies the story's existence. Picoult's version earns its devastation — Cassavetes' asks you to cry without doing the philosophical work.
