The Story in Brief

Eleven-year-old Mark Sway stumbles into the woods near his Memphis trailer park with his younger brother Ricky to smoke cigarettes, and witnesses mob attorney Jerome Clifford shoot himself in the head — but not before Clifford, drunk and suicidal, reveals where mafia hitman Barry Muldanno buried Senator Boyd Boyette's corpse. Ricky goes catatonic from the trauma. Mark lies to the FBI, hires alcoholic divorce lawyer Reggie Love for a dollar, and finds himself caught between U.S. Attorney Roy Foltrigg, who needs the body's location to make his career, and the Muldanno family, who'll kill Mark to keep him quiet. Grisham turns this premise into a 400-page examination of how institutions — the FBI, the mafia, the family court system, the media — devour children who can't fight back.

Joel Schumacher's 1994 adaptation came fast — the book topped bestseller lists for months, and Warner Bros. wanted their Grisham franchise locked in after The Firm's success. Susan Sarandon took the Reggie Love role and an Oscar nomination with it. Tommy Lee Jones played Foltrigg as a preening Southern dandy. Brad Renfro, in his film debut at age eleven, played Mark with a feral wariness that kept the movie grounded when Schumacher's tendency toward gloss threatened to prettify the poverty Grisham insists on. The film earned $117 million worldwide and sits comfortably in the middle tier of Grisham adaptations — competent, star-driven, and too polite to let the ugliness breathe.

Key Differences

Mark's Family and the Economics of Desperation

Grisham's novel makes the Sway family's poverty inescapable. Mark's mother Dianne works two jobs — days at a lamp factory, nights waitressing — and still can't afford Ricky's hospital bills after his breakdown. Mark shoplifts food. The family lives in a trailer with a broken air conditioner in a park where the other residents are meth cooks and welfare cases. When Reggie tries to move them to Portland for witness protection, Mark resists because his mother has finally lined up a third job that might cover the bills. The entire plot turns on the fact that Mark can't simply leave Memphis — his family is trapped by debt, and he knows it. Schumacher keeps the trailer and shows Dianne chain-smoking in the hospital, but the financial desperation becomes set dressing. We never see the lamp factory. The film skips the scene where Mark calculates exactly how many shifts his mother will lose if they relocate. Dianne, played by Mary-Louise Parker, cries and hugs her son but never voices the arithmetic of survival that makes Mark's loyalty so costly. The movie wants our sympathy for the Sways without forcing us to watch them count coins.

Reggie Love's Backstory and Why She Takes the Case

The novel gives Reggie Love sixty pages of history. She was Regina Cardoni, married to a doctor who beat her and destroyed her confidence. She had a breakdown, spent time in a psychiatric facility, got sober, went to law school at forty, and built a practice representing battered women and kids in custody fights. She hates the FBI because they bungled a case that got one of her clients killed. She takes Mark's case for a dollar because she sees herself in him — a child the system wants to use and discard. Grisham shows her attending AA meetings, managing her rage, and calculating exactly how far she'll go to protect Mark before she loses her license. Sarandon's Reggie is warmer and more instinctively maternal. The film includes one scene where she mentions her abusive ex-husband while sitting on a porch swing with Mark, and that's the extent of it. We don't see the AA meetings. We don't see her law partner Clint monitoring her stress levels. Sarandon plays Reggie as a crusader who takes the case because it's the right thing to do, not because Mark's terror mirrors her own. The performance is excellent — Sarandon makes every scene hum with decency — but the character is now a hero rather than a survivor choosing to fight one more time.

Roy Foltrigg's Media Circus and Senate Ambitions

Grisham writes Foltrigg as a U.S. Attorney who stages press conferences in three-piece suits and rehearses his sound bites in the mirror. He wants Mark's testimony because the Boyette case will get him on CNN and position him for a Senate run. The novel includes scenes of Foltrigg polling his staff on which tie to wear, dictating quotes to journalists, and fantasizing about his confirmation hearings. He's a bureaucrat who sees Mark as a prop. Tommy Lee Jones turns him into a comic villain — vain, yes, but also buffoonish. Jones plays Foltrigg with an affected drawl and a prissy waddle, and Schumacher shoots him in low angles to emphasize his ridiculousness. The film cuts the polling scenes and the Senate ambitions. We get Foltrigg's vanity but not the institutional machinery that rewards it. In the book, Foltrigg represents a system that turns children into evidence. In the film, he's a bad boss, and the movie lets us laugh at him rather than recognize him.

The Mafia's Surveillance and Escalation

Grisham devotes entire chapters to Barry Muldanno's crew tracking Mark. They bug Reggie's office. They follow Mark to school. They burn down the Sway trailer — an act the novel treats as terrorism, complete with Mark watching the only home he's ever known reduced to ash. The hitman Paul Grogu corners Mark in a hospital elevator and puts a switchblade to his ribs, whispering exactly how he'll kill Dianne and Ricky if Mark talks. Later, Grogu nearly abducts Mark from a hospital stairwell, and Mark escapes only because a nurse interrupts. The book makes it clear that the mafia is patient, methodical, and willing to kill a child. Schumacher includes the elevator knife threat but cuts the trailer burning entirely — the Sways are still in their home at the end. The film shortens the surveillance sequences and makes the mafia less omnipresent. We see thugs in a car, but we don't feel them as the constant predatory presence Grisham describes. The movie wants suspense without the sustained dread of a child being hunted.

The Ending and What Mark Chooses

Grisham's novel ends with Mark leading the FBI to Senator Boyette's body — buried in Clifford's garage in New Orleans — and then disappearing into witness protection with his family. They're relocated to Phoenix with new names. Mark reflects that he'll never see his friends again, his mother will never see her sister, and Ricky will grow up not remembering Memphis. The final chapter shows Mark in his new school, still looking over his shoulder, knowing the mafia has a long memory. It's a survival, not a victory. Schumacher ends with Mark and Reggie walking out of the courthouse together, Mark smiling, the family still in Memphis. The film includes a title card saying the Sways entered witness protection, but we don't see it. We don't see Mark saying goodbye to his old life. The movie gives us a freeze-frame of Sarandon and Renfro's friendship, triumphant and intact, because it wants us to leave the theater feeling good. Grisham wants us to leave knowing that Mark survived by losing everything familiar, and that the cost of testimony is exile.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first if you want to understand why Mark Sway doesn't simply tell the FBI where the body is buried — Grisham makes it clear that Mark's silence isn't stubbornness but calculation born of poverty and survival instinct. The novel's first hundred pages are Mark weighing his options, knowing that cooperating means his family loses their home and his mother loses her jobs, while refusing means the mafia kills them all. The film, starting with Schumacher's glossier aesthetic and Sarandon's warmth, feels like a boy and his lawyer beating the system. You'll enjoy it more that way. But you'll miss the economic trap that makes Mark's situation desperate rather than adventurous, and you'll miss the ending's bitterness — the recognition that witness protection is just a polite term for erasing a child's life.

If you watch first, you'll get a lean, star-powered thriller with one of Brad Renfro's best performances and Sarandon at her most compelling. You won't notice what's missing because the movie works on its own terms. But when you read the book afterward, you'll realize Schumacher turned a novel about institutional cruelty into a story about good people winning, and you'll feel the absence of every scene Grisham wrote to show that winning, in this case, just means surviving.

Verdict

The book gives you the economics that make Mark's choice impossible, the mafia that burns his home, and the ending where survival means exile. The film gives you Sarandon's decency, Jones's preening villain, and a finale where the kid walks free. Grisham's version is the one that lingers because it refuses to pretend Mark escapes unscathed.
