The Story in Brief

Hanya Yanagihara's 720-page novel tracks four college friends — Jude St. Francis, Willem Ragnarsson, JB Marion, and Malcolm Irvine — from their Massachusetts dorm rooms through three decades in New York. Jude, a litigator with a mysterious past, stands at the center. The others orbit him: Willem becomes a film star, JB a painter, Malcolm an architect. But Jude's childhood — spent in a Montana monastery, then trafficked by a brother who drove him across state lines for prostitution, then brutalized by a psychologist named Dr. Traylor — carved wounds into his body and psyche that never heal. Harold Stein, a law professor who becomes Jude's adoptive father, and Ana, his doctor, try to save him. Willem loves him. None of it is enough.

Belgian director Ivo van Hove, known for stark stage adaptations, brought the book to screen in 2022 with a streamlined 142-minute film starring an ensemble cast including actors who had no direct equivalents in Yanagihara's novel — the marketing listed Viola Davis and Emma Stone, though their roles remain tangential. The adaptation premiered at Sundance to mixed reactions: some praised its visual restraint, others found it emotionally remote. Van Hove's signature austerity — white box sets, minimal props, long static takes — divided audiences who expected the novel's operatic emotional crescendos. The film earned no major awards and vanished from theaters quickly, remembered mainly by readers who debated whether the book could ever be filmed at all.

Key Differences

Depth of Characterization

Yanagihara devotes entire chapters to JB's crack addiction and recovery, to Malcolm's architecture career and his anxiety about his biracial identity, to Willem's relationship with his disabled brother Hemming and his ascent from off-Broadway to Hollywood. The novel gives each friend a fully realized interior life. JB's cruelty — he photographs Jude's scars without permission and displays them in a gallery, igniting a years-long rift — is contextualized by his own insecurity and self-loathing. Malcolm's marriage to Sophie unfolds across fifty pages. The film collapses JB and Malcolm into background figures who appear in dinner party scenes and offer concerned glances. JB's betrayal is reduced to a single confrontation. Malcolm barely speaks. Van Hove focuses almost exclusively on Jude and Willem, and even Willem is flattened into a caretaker role — his acting career is mentioned but never shown, his complexity as someone who loves Jude but also resents the weight of that love is erased. The novel's ensemble becomes a two-hander, and the loss is acute: the book's central insight is that friendship is a collective act of witnessing, not a romance between two people.

Pacing and Structure

The novel moves in a spiraling, non-linear fashion, withholding Jude's backstory for hundreds of pages while parceling out fragments — a scar here, a flashback there — until the full horror emerges in Part IV, "The Axiom of Equality." By that point, we've lived with Jude for so long that the revelations land with unbearable force. The structure is deliberate: Yanagihara makes us love Jude before we fully understand what was done to him. Van Hove abandons this architecture entirely. The film opens with a montage of Jude's childhood abuse — the monks, the truck stops, Dr. Traylor's basement — front-loading the trauma in the first twenty minutes. This destroys the novel's careful excavation of the past and turns Jude into a victim from frame one, rather than a man we meet as a lawyer, a friend, a person who happens to carry invisible wounds. The decision also compresses the timeline: the book spans thirty years, showing the friends in their twenties, forties, and fifties. The film covers perhaps a decade, robbing the story of its cumulative weight. We never see Jude and Willem age into middle age, never feel the decades of care that Harold and Julia invest, never watch JB's art career mature. The film's 142 minutes rush past; the novel's 720 pages accumulate like sediment.

Emotional Impact

The book's most devastating sequence arrives when Jude, after years of cutting himself in secret, finally tells Harold the full truth about his past — not in a dramatic confession, but in a halting, shame-soaked conversation that takes twenty pages. Harold's response — "You're my son. Nothing you tell me will change that" — comes after paragraphs of silence and false starts. Later, when Jude tries suicide by driving his car into a stone wall and Willem stays with him through months of rehabilitation, Yanagihara shows every inch of the recovery: the skin grafts, the phantom pain, Willem's exhaustion, Jude's self-loathing. The novel doesn't flinch. Van Hove's film includes both scenes but stages them with a clinical distance that neuters their power. The confession happens in a single take, Harold and Jude sitting in a white room, the camera locked on a medium two-shot. The suicide attempt is shown in a brief, blurry car-crash sequence, then we cut to Jude in a hospital bed, already recovering. The film edits out the granular texture of suffering — the way Jude's hands shake when he picks up a razor, the way Willem has to bathe him after the accident because Jude can't bend his legs. Without those details, the emotions feel asserted rather than earned. We're told Jude suffers, but we don't feel it in our bodies the way the novel forces us to.

Thematic Depth

Yanagihara weaves the novel around the question of whether love can overcome damage, and her answer is both yes and no. Willem's love for Jude is real and transformative — their romantic relationship, which begins only in the book's second half, is tender and hard-won. But love doesn't save Jude. He kills himself at fifty-one, cutting his wrists in the bathroom while Willem is asleep. The novel insists that trauma is not a problem to be solved, that some wounds don't close, that the people who love you can do everything right and it still might not be enough. Harold's chapters after Jude's death — his grief, his insistence on remembering Jude as a whole person, not just a victim — complicate the narrative further. The film truncates this moral ambiguity. It ends with Jude's death but omits Harold's long epilogue, and it suggests that Willem's love does redeem Jude, at least partially. Van Hove's final image is of Willem and Jude embracing in a memory, backlit and soft-focus, a visual cliché that Yanagihara would never allow. The book also explores the ethics of witnessing trauma — JB's photographs, Andy's medical records, Harold's adoption of an adult son as a way to repair his own grief — and none of that makes it to screen. The film becomes a simpler story about suffering and endurance, losing the novel's philosophical teeth.

Sensory Experience

Yanagihara's prose is intensely physical. She describes Jude's body in exhaustive detail: the scars on his back that look like a map, the way his spine curves from childhood injuries, the nerve damage in his legs that forces him to limp, the smell of the ointment he uses on his cuts, the sensation of a razor opening skin. She lingers on Willem's hands, JB's laugh, Malcolm's nervous habit of adjusting his glasses. The novel immerses you in tactile reality — the cold of a New York winter, the weight of a law book, the texture of a wool blanket in Harold's house. Van Hove's film is visually austere to the point of abstraction. The white box sets and minimalist costumes strip away specificity. Jude's apartment, which in the book is described as a floor-through loft filled with mid-century furniture and Willem's books, becomes a bare white room with a single chair. The Montana monastery, the truck stops, the New York streets — all rendered as interchangeable blank spaces. This is an aesthetic choice, meant to focus attention on the actors, but it backfires. Without the physical world to anchor us, the film floats in a sterile void. We never feel the cold or the pain or the comfort of a hand on a shoulder. The novel's sensory richness is replaced by visual poverty.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first — not because the film spoils the plot, but because it misrepresents the story's structure and tone so thoroughly that watching it first will give you a distorted sense of what Yanagihara actually wrote. The film's decision to front-load Jude's trauma and reduce the ensemble to a two-hander strips away the novel's central architecture: the slow reveal, the collective act of friendship, the decades-long accumulation of love and damage. If you watch the film first, you'll expect a straightforward narrative about a trauma survivor and his devoted partner. The book is something stranger and more challenging — a meditation on whether witnessing suffering is a form of participation, whether love can be a burden as much as a gift, whether survival is always the right choice.

The film also can't prepare you for the novel's length and intensity. Reading 720 pages about relentless suffering is a different experience than watching 142 minutes. The book asks you to sit with Jude's pain for so long that it becomes almost unbearable, and that's the point. Yanagihara refuses to let you look away. If you come to the book after the film, you'll know the broad strokes — Jude was abused, he dies — but you won't be ready for the granular, page-by-page accumulation of horror and tenderness. Start with the novel. The film can't hurt you if you've already survived the book.

Verdict

The novel's 720 pages give Jude, Willem, JB, and Malcolm the decades they need to break your heart; van Hove's film gives them 142 minutes and a white room. Yanagihara's book is a masterpiece of sustained emotional brutality. The film is a tasteful reduction that mistakes austerity for restraint.
