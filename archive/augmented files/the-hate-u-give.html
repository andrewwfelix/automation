The Story in Brief

Sixteen-year-old Starr Carter lives in Garden Heights, a Black neighborhood scarred by poverty and gang violence, but commutes daily to Williamson Prep, a predominantly white private school where she code-switches to avoid seeming "too hood" around classmates like her boyfriend Chris. After attending a spring break party in Garden Heights, Starr accepts a ride home from Khalil Harris, her childhood friend and former crush. When Officer Brian Cruise Jr. pulls them over for a broken taillight and shoots Khalil — unarmed, holding only a hairbrush — Starr becomes the sole witness to a killing that ignites protests, media scrutiny, and a grand jury investigation. She must decide whether to testify, risking retaliation from King, the gang leader who used Khalil as a drug runner, while navigating her parents Maverick and Lisa's fears, her Uncle Carlos's perspective as a Black cop, and the willful ignorance of white Williamson students who treat Khalil's death as a debate topic.

Angie Thomas's debut novel arrived in February 2017, months before the film rights sold to Fox 2000, and became a sustained number-one New York Times bestseller — unusual stamina for YA that reflected the book's resonance during ongoing Black Lives Matter activism. George Tillman Jr.'s 2018 adaptation, with Amandla Stenberg as Starr and Audrey Wells adapting the screenplay shortly before her death, opened to $29 million and strong reviews that praised Stenberg's performance. The film became one of the rare YA adaptations to earn both critical approval and commercial success without a franchise apparatus, cementing the story's place in the post-Ferguson cultural conversation about police violence and the cost of speaking up.

Key Differences

Starr's Interior Code-Switching

Thomas structures the novel around Starr's constant mental translation between Garden Heights vernacular and Williamson-acceptable speech — a process the prose tracks sentence by sentence. When Starr sits in class the Monday after Khalil's death, she narrates how she monitors her reactions: "I can't be too loud or I'm 'that angry Black girl.' I can't be too quiet or they'll think something's wrong with me." The film shows Stenberg switching vocal registers between home and school, but can't convey the exhausting self-surveillance that Thomas renders through Starr's first-person accounting. We see Starr change how she speaks, but not the split-second calculations behind each adjustment — which classmates will tolerate slang, which moments require her to dial down anger, when she's allowed to claim Garden Heights versus when she must distance herself from it. The book makes code-switching a cognitive load; the film makes it a performance choice.

This gap matters most in the Williamson Prep scenes after a grand jury declines to indict Officer Cruise. In the novel, Starr's white classmate Hailey suggests Khalil "was a drug dealer anyway," and Starr's internal monologue spirals through whether confronting Hailey will mark her as the "angry Black girl" stereotype, whether Chris will support her or pull back, whether her teacher Mr. Warren will mediate or scold her for disrupting class. Thomas gives us three pages of Starr's racing thoughts before she finally snaps and throws Hailey's yogurt. The film condenses this to a quick escalation — Hailey's comment, Starr's immediate reaction, the cafeteria fight. We lose Starr's dread that speaking up will confirm every assumption Williamson students already hold, the cost-benefit analysis she runs in real time. The result is that the film's Starr seems reactive, while the book's Starr is reactive after prolonged, painful deliberation.

Maverick's Gang Past and Parenting Philosophy

Common plays Maverick as a protective father in the film, but the script reduces his backstory to a few lines about prison time. Thomas dedicates chapters to Maverick's history with the King Lords — how he joined at twelve when his own father was absent, spent three years in prison for taking the fall on a drug charge to protect King, and chose to leave the gang after Starr was born. The novel shows Maverick teaching Starr and her brothers Sekani and Seven the "Black Panther Ten-Point Program" at breakfast, a ritual that establishes his political consciousness and his belief that Garden Heights deserves investment rather than abandonment. When Maverick refuses to move the family to a safer suburb, Thomas gives him a full-chapter explanation: leaving would mean ceding Garden Heights to King, admitting that Black neighborhoods are disposable, failing to model staying and rebuilding. The film gestures at this in a single dinner-table exchange, cutting the King Lords history that makes Maverick's decision risky — King still views him as a deserter who owes loyalty.

This truncation flattens Maverick's moral position. In the book, his refusal to flee isn't naive idealism; it's a calculated stance that he argues with Lisa over multiple scenes, weighing their children's safety against the message that Garden Heights families should self-exile. Thomas also details Maverick's grocery store job and his small business loans for the store he opens mid-novel, showing how he enacts the self-determination he preaches. The film montages his store opening into a brief success sequence. We see the ribbon-cutting, not the loan applications Maverick describes in the book — the white bank officers who doubt Garden Heights' viability, his pitch that the neighborhood needs a Black-owned market selling fresh produce instead of corner-store junk food. Common delivers the film's big Maverick speech — the "Thug Life" explainer — with appropriate gravity, but without the accumulated detail Thomas provides, it plays as a single moment of wisdom rather than the culmination of a lifetime philosophy Maverick has been teaching his children every day.

Uncle Carlos and the Blue Wall of Silence

Carlos, Lisa's brother and a police detective, occupies moral no-man's-land in Thomas's novel — a Black cop who loves Starr but initially defends the procedures that justify Khalil's shooting. The book stages a searing argument at Carlos's house where he walks Starr through Officer Cruise's perspective: Khalil reached back into the car, Cruise feared for his life, the hairbrush could have been a gun. Starr counters every point — Khalil was following Cruise's orders to show his hands, Cruise never asked him to freeze before shooting, the assumption that a Black boy reaching equals a threat is the problem. Thomas gives this debate four pages of escalating intensity, ending with Carlos admitting he once pulled his weapon on a jaywalking Black teenager and has been justifying that moment to himself ever since. It's a confession that cracks Carlos's cop persona and forces him to choose whether he's a police officer who happens to be Black or a Black man who happens to be a police officer.

The film includes the argument, but Corey Hawkins and Stenberg play it as a shorter, less serrated exchange. Carlos defends Cruise's fear for one scene, then pivots to supporting Starr's testimony without the prolonged cognitive dissonance Thomas charts. The novel returns to Carlos's internal conflict repeatedly — he's pressured by white colleagues who expect him to denounce the Garden Heights protests, he tells Starr he's been called "nigger" by officers in his own precinct, he finally admits that the police institution prioritizes protecting officers over admitting wrongdoing. Thomas uses Carlos to dramatize how institutional loyalty corrodes personal morality. The film simplifies Carlos into a supportive uncle who wrestles with his job briefly, then sides with family — a cleaner arc, but one that loses Thomas's indictment of how police culture demands that Black officers choose complicity or isolation.

The Grand Jury Testimony Sequence

Starr's testimony before the grand jury occupies twenty pages in the novel. Thomas puts the reader in the windowless room where Starr faces twelve jurors and a prosecutor who treats Officer Cruise as the victim needing protection. The DA asks Starr whether Khalil sold drugs, whether he was a gang member, whether he made "sudden movements" — questions designed to put Khalil on trial instead of Cruise. Starr's internal narration tracks her realization that the proceeding is theater: the DA's tone softens when asking about Khalil's behavior and hardens when she tries to describe Cruise's aggression. Thomas includes the DA's closing statement to the jury, a speech that reframes the shooting as a tragedy for Officer Cruise, who must live with having taken a life while doing his job. Starr listens to Khalil being eulogized as a threat and Cruise as a haunted hero, then learns the grand jury declined to indict.

The film reduces the testimony to a montage under Starr's voiceover, cutting from her entering the courthouse to her exit. We don't see the hostile questioning, the jury's faces, the DA's reframing of the case. The decision not to indict arrives via a TV news broadcast that Starr watches at home. What the book treats as the procedural rigging of justice — the way grand juries in police shooting cases are stage-managed to protect officers — becomes a disappointing but unsurprising outcome in the film. Thomas wants the reader to witness the machinery of exoneration in granular detail; Tillman opts for a more elliptical approach that keeps the pacing tighter but sacrifices the didactic force. The book makes you sit through the injustice minute by minute. The film acknowledges it happened and moves to the community's response.

The Riot and Maverick's Store

When the grand jury's decision is announced, Garden Heights erupts. Thomas choreographs the riot across three chapters — the initial protests outside the police station, the burning of a patrol car, the tear gas deployment, the crowd's migration to Magnolia Avenue where Maverick's store sits. Starr, Seven, and Chris drive into the riot searching for Sekani, encountering overturned dumpsters, looters breaking into stores, and neighbors forming chains to protect Black-owned businesses. When King's crew throws a Molotov cocktail into Maverick's store — revenge for Maverick's refusal to help King launder drug money — Thomas slows the scene to capture Maverick's face as he watches his investment burn. The store represents Maverick's thesis that Garden Heights can rebuild itself; King torching it is a repudiation of that belief, a statement that gang economics trump legitimate business. Thomas ends the chapter with Starr, Maverick, and Lisa standing in the store's ashes, Maverick silent, before he finally says they'll rebuild.

The film compresses the riot into a shorter sequence with less attention to the store's symbolic weight. We see the tear gas, the overturned cars, the fire — but Maverick's store burns in a quick succession of shots without the lingering devastation Thomas stages. The film also adds a moment where neighborhood residents help extinguish the flames, a grace note of community solidarity that the book doesn't include. Thomas's version is harsher: the store burns completely, some neighbors watch without intervening, and Maverick's silence conveys that his experiment in Black economic self-determination has been destroyed by the same forces he hoped to transcend. The film's more hopeful imagery — neighbors with hoses, Starr's voiceover affirming that Garden Heights will rise again — softens the blow. The book treats the fire as an unresolved wound; the film treats it as a setback the community is already overcoming.

Should You Read First?

Read the book before the film if you want to understand why Starr hesitates so long before testifying — Thomas maps every fear, every weighing of risk, every accommodation Starr has made to survive in white spaces, which makes her final decision to speak up earned rather than inevitable. The film's Starr moves from trauma to activism more cleanly because Stenberg can't voice the endless internal negotiation Thomas writes. Watching first will give you a capable, emotionally coherent version of the story; reading first will show you the self-protective calculations the film has no tool to dramatize. The film assumes you'll fill in Starr's thought process. The book is Starr's thought process.

The other reason to read first: Thomas doesn't flinch from showing how Garden Heights' problems are structural, not soluble by one girl's testimony. Maverick's store burns, King remains powerful, Officer Cruise faces no consequences, and Starr's final act — using her witness statement as ammunition in a TV interview — is resistance, not resolution. The film gives Starr a closing voiceover about finding her voice and fighting for change, language that implies progress. The book ends with Starr driving past Khalil's memorial, knowing his death will be forgotten by everyone outside Garden Heights within weeks. If you watch first, you'll think the story is about Starr's empowerment. If you read first, you'll recognize the story is about the machinery that kills Khalils and how a single witness can't dismantle it, only refuse to be silent within it.

Verdict

The book shows you how Starr performs respectability politics at Williamson, then dismantles that performance piece by piece until she's willing to explode it on television — a process Thomas traces across 400 pages that the film can't compress without simplifying. Stenberg is incandescent and the film does right by the major beats, but Thomas wrote a manual on code-switching and the film adapted a story about finding your voice. Both matter; one cuts deeper.
