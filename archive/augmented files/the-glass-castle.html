The Story in Brief

Jeannette Walls' 2005 memoir recounts her chaotic childhood with Rex and Rose Mary Walls, parents who valued freedom and art over basic necessities like food and shelter. Rex, an alcoholic dreamer, promises his four children a glass castle he'll build in the desert once he strikes it rich — a fantasy that never materializes as the family moves from town to town, often living without electricity or plumbing. Rose Mary, a trained teacher who refuses steady work, paints and writes while her children scavenge through garbage for meals. Jeannette and her siblings — Lori, Brian, and Maureen — eventually escape to New York City, where Jeannette becomes a successful journalist, though she hides her past until her parents, now homeless in Manhattan, force a reckoning with where she came from.

Director Destin Daniel Cretton's 2017 adaptation casts Brie Larson as adult Jeannette and Woody Harrelson as Rex, structuring the story around Jeannette's 1989 engagement party, where flashbacks reveal her childhood in West Virginia and Arizona. The film softens the memoir's harshest edges — particularly Rose Mary's neglect and Rex's violence — and frames the story as Jeannette's journey to forgive her parents rather than simply survive them. Despite a strong performance from Harrelson, who captures Rex's charisma and self-destruction, the film earned mixed reviews and underwhelmed at the box office, criticized for sanitizing a story whose power lies in its refusal to sentimentalize poverty or excuse parental failure.

Key Differences

Narrative Structure

Walls' memoir opens with adult Jeannette spotting her mother digging through a dumpster on Park Avenue, then rewinds to her earliest memory — cooking hot dogs at age three and catching fire, a scene that establishes both the family's neglect and Jeannette's resourcefulness. The book moves chronologically through childhood but constantly circles back to adult perspective, Jeannette reflecting on moments she couldn't fully understand as a child. When Rex takes her to a bar at age ten and lets men dance with her for money, the adult narrator doesn't editorialize — she simply reports what happened and lets the reader draw conclusions. This structure creates a tension between the child who loves her father and the adult who recognizes his failures.

Cretton's film uses Jeannette's engagement party as a framing device, cutting between 1989 Manhattan and her childhood memories. The party scenes — Jeannette awkwardly introducing her wealthy fiancé David to her estranged father — interrupt the chronological flashbacks and shift the focus from survival to reconciliation. The film invents a climactic moment where adult Jeannette confronts Rex about letting a stranger molest her in exchange for cash, a scene that prioritizes catharsis over the memoir's ambiguity. By jumping between timelines, the film positions adult Jeannette as the emotional center rather than the resilient child, which fundamentally changes whose story this is. The book trusts readers to sit with discomfort; the film needs to resolve it.

Character Development

In the memoir, Rex Walls emerges through accumulated detail — teaching Jeannette physics and astronomy, then spending grocery money on whiskey; defending her against bullies, then disappearing for days on drinking binges. Walls includes the scene where Rex, needing cash, pimps young Jeannette to a child molester named Robbie, then interrupts before anything happens, as if this makes him heroic. She records his N-word-laden rants, his violence toward Rose Mary, and the Christmas when he "gave" Jeannette a star because he had no money for gifts. The book never tells you whether to forgive Rex — it presents evidence and lets you decide. Rose Mary comes off even worse: she hides a giant Hershey bar and eats it in front of her starving children, owns a valuable piece of land in Texas but refuses to sell it, and tells Jeannette that being sexually assaulted will make her "more compassionate."

Harrelson's Rex is gentler, more obviously loving despite his flaws. The film includes the star scene and the hot-dog cooking but cuts Robbie entirely, replacing him with a vague reference to "bad men" Rex protected Jeannette from. It omits Rose Mary's chocolate-hoarding and reduces her land ownership to a brief mention, eliminating the detail that she chooses homelessness over selling an asset. Naomi Watts plays Rose Mary as charmingly eccentric rather than willfully neglectful — her refusal to work becomes artistic integrity, not selfishness. The film adds scenes of Rex building part of the glass castle's foundation and teaching Jeannette to swim by throwing her in a pool then rescuing her, framing these as tough-love parenting rather than reckless endangerment. By making the parents more sympathetic, the film simplifies Jeannette's conflict: it's easier to forgive flawed heroes than accept that the people who harmed you also loved you.

Emotional Resonance

The memoir's power comes from Walls' flat, almost journalistic tone when describing horrors. She writes that in Welch, West Virginia, the family's house had no insulation, so they wore multiple coats indoors and slept under piles of blankets, and the ceiling leaked so badly they put buckets everywhere — then she moves on, no melodrama. When Jeannette gets jumped by neighborhood girls and comes home bleeding, Rose Mary tells her to "show more humility." When Brian finds a diamond ring and Rex immediately sells it for drinking money, Walls reports this in a single sentence. The restraint makes the abuse more devastating — you feel her training herself not to expect better. The book's emotional climax isn't a confrontation but Jeannette's adult realization that she's ashamed of her parents, juxtaposed with her memory of Rex promising her the glass castle, and the reader has to hold both truths simultaneously.

Cretton's film underlines emotions the book leaves implicit. Swelling strings accompany Rex's astronomy lessons. When young Jeannette burns herself cooking, the camera lingers on her screaming in pain, making sure we register the trauma. The engagement party scenes feature Brie Larson's face contorting with suppressed rage and grief, telling us she's conflicted in case we missed it. The film's climax — the invented confrontation where adult Jeannette yells "You let him touch me!" — gives her a cathartic outburst the real Walls never claims to have had. The final scene shows a reconciliation dinner where Jeannette toasts Rex before his death, reframing her story as one of forgiveness achieved rather than ambivalence endured. By making the emotions explicit and resolving them neatly, the film offers comfort where the book offers complexity. It's the difference between being told how to feel and having to figure out what you feel.

Pacing and Tone

Walls' memoir has an accelerating structure — early chapters dwell on specific incidents, like the family's escape from the law in California or the years spent in Phoenix living in relative stability. The tone shifts from almost whimsical adventure story to something darker as the family moves to Welch and conditions deteriorate. The Phoenix section spans dozens of pages, but the Welch years, where Jeannette experiences her worst deprivation and abuse, rush by in shorter, more fragmented chapters, as if the child is dissociating. The book's second half, covering the children's escapes to New York and their adult lives, takes up only a third of the page count, reflecting how the trauma concentrates in childhood. This uneven pacing mirrors how memory works — some years blur together, other moments freeze in detail.

The film distributes its runtime more evenly across periods, giving equal weight to the Arizona years and the New York timeline. It lingers on visually beautiful scenes — Rex teaching Jeannette to stargaze, the family's Southwest road trips — and rushes through uglier material. The Welch section, which dominates the memoir's second half, gets compressed into a montage of poverty punctuated by a few key scenes. Moments that Walls narrates in a few sentences get expanded into full sequences with dialogue and music. The film adds a late scene where Rex, newly sober, apologizes to Jeannette, an invention that shifts the tone from tragic to redemptive. By evening out the pacing and ending on reconciliation, the film turns a story about surviving your childhood into one about healing from it — a more conventionally satisfying arc that smooths over the memoir's jagged edges.

Thematic Depth

The memoir examines how poverty and neglect coexist with love, and how children convince themselves their deprivation is adventure. Jeannette describes "skedaddling" — the family's term for fleeing town to avoid creditors — as exciting until she's old enough to recognize it as homelessness. She writes about "the Rex Walls Plan," her father's schemes that never pan out, and how long she believed in them. The book asks whether Rex's lessons about self-reliance and questioning authority justify the hunger and danger he exposed his children to, and Walls never answers — she just shows that she escaped, while her sister Maureen, the youngest, ended up mentally ill and estranged. The memoir also explores class and shame: adult Jeannette hides her past from colleagues, and when she finally publishes her story, she's exposing a secret she spent decades concealing. The book is as much about the cost of upward mobility as about surviving poverty.

The film foregrounds forgiveness and resilience, themes the memoir complicates rather than celebrates. It emphasizes Rex's intelligence and his teachings about physics, framing him as a misunderstood genius rather than an alcoholic who endangered his children. Rose Mary's artistic aspirations get more screen time than her refusals to feed her kids. The class dynamics are simplified — adult Jeannette's wealthy Manhattan life is presented as straightforward success, not something she's ambivalent about. The film cuts Maureen's story entirely; she appears as a child but her adult breakdown never comes up, erasing the evidence that not all the Walls children escaped intact. By ending with Jeannette reconciling with Rex before his death, the film suggests that understanding your parents' damage is the same as healing from your own. The memoir knows those are different things. It's a book about what it costs to survive your family; the film is about learning to love them anyway.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first, because the film's softening of Rex and Rose Mary will seem reasonable only if you don't know what Cretton left out. Walls' memoir includes Robbie the child molester, Rose Mary eating chocolate while her children starve, and Rex's racist tirades — details the film omits or downplays. If you watch the movie first, Woody Harrelson's Rex seems like a charming drunk who loves his kids but can't get it together, and you'll read the memoir wondering why Walls is being so hard on him. Then you'll hit the scene where he knowingly lets a pedophile take young Jeannette upstairs, and you'll realize the film protected you from the book's actual content. Reading first preserves the memoir's refusal to sentimentalize — Walls never says "despite everything, I loved them," because the book is about the impossibility of that "despite." The film tries to resolve contradictions the memoir insists on living inside.

If you watch first, you'll also expect a reconciliation the book never provides. Cretton invents a confrontation and an apology that let adult Jeannette and Rex achieve closure; Walls writes that she saw her father in a VA hospital before he died and felt "conflicted." The film's invented ending will make the book's actual ambiguity feel unsatisfying, as if Walls failed to finish her own story. But that ambivalence is the point — she never stops loving her father and never forgives what he did, and both things are true. Reading first lets you sit with that discomfort. Watching first teaches you to expect resolution, then punishes you for wanting it.

Verdict

The book wins because Walls reports what happened and trusts you to handle it, while Cretton softens the abuse, invents a reconciliation, and cuts the scenes that make Rex and Rose Mary unforgivable. Harrelson is excellent, but he's playing a gentler man than the one in the memoir.
