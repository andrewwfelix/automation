The Story in Brief

Fourteen-year-old Lily Owens escapes her abusive father T. Ray in Sylvan, South Carolina, in the summer of 1964, carrying only a wooden picture of a Black Madonna and her father's housekeeper Rosaleen, who has been beaten for attempting to register to vote. They land in Tiburon, where Lily discovers the Boatwright sisters — August, June, and May — who keep bees, make Black Madonna Honey, and worship a wooden statue of a black Virgin Mary in their bright pink house. August becomes Lily's surrogate mother, teaching her beekeeping while Lily hides the truth about her own mother's death, which she caused accidentally as a four-year-old. June initially resents Lily's presence, May's hypersensitivity to the world's suffering threatens to overwhelm her, and Zach, August's handsome godson, becomes Lily's first love despite the deadly danger of interracial romance in Jim Crow South Carolina.

Sue Monk Kidd's debut novel became a word-of-mouth sensation, spending over two years on the New York Times bestseller list and selling more than six million copies before Fox Searchlight greenlit the adaptation. Director Gina Prince-Bythewood assembled a formidable cast — Queen Latifah as August, Alicia Keys as June, Sophie Okonedo as May, Dakota Fanning as Lily, and Jennifer Hudson fresh from her Dreamgirls Oscar — but the film opened to mixed reviews and modest box office in October 2008. Critics praised the performances, particularly Okonedo's haunting turn as the fragile May, but questioned whether the story's careful treatment of racial reconciliation had curdled into something too palatable for white audiences seeking absolution without confrontation.

Key Differences

Lily's Interior Life and Guilt

Kidd's novel grants full access to Lily's obsessive internal monologue about killing her mother — the gun, the noise, the moment Deborah fell. She catalogs her father's cruelties in forensic detail: the kneeling on Martha White grits, his insistence that Deborah was leaving Lily behind, not T. Ray, when she died. Lily's self-loathing surfaces in phrases like "the woman who died because of me" and her conviction that she is fundamentally unlovable. Dakota Fanning plays Lily's guilt more softly, with teary close-ups and a few stammered confessions, but the film eliminates most of her caustic self-assessment. We lose the passages where Lily imagines scenarios of her mother's departure, turning each memory over like a puzzle piece. The book's Lily is sardonic and angry; Fanning's Lily is wistful and bewildered. When August finally tells Lily that Deborah did leave her and came back only to retrieve her belongings, the revelation lands with less devastation in the film because we haven't spent two hours inside Lily's desperate mythmaking about her mother's unconditional love.

May Boatwright's Wailing Wall and Suicide

May's ritual of writing down every grief on paper scraps and tucking them into her backyard wailing wall — a stone structure she built with August after her twin April's suicide — appears in both versions, but the novel gives us months of May's notes: names of Black children bombed in Birmingham, a neighbor's cancer diagnosis, the death of a dog hit by a car. Kidd shows how May absorbs the world's pain without filter, singing "Oh! Susanna" to calm herself, a coping mechanism that finally fails when Zach is arrested and jailed for a crime he didn't commit. Sophie Okonedo captures May's trembling fragility beautifully, but the film compresses her breakdown and drowning to a single sequence that plays like inevitable tragedy rather than the slow, accretive weight the novel describes. The book devotes pages to the sisters finding May's body in the river, her pockets full of stones like Virginia Woolf, and the note she leaves explaining that she wants to be with April and can't carry any more sorrow. The film finds May's body at dusk in a scene that lasts maybe ninety seconds. We don't see the sisters read her letter aloud or sit with her body through the night. The funeral and August's decision to take off her mourning clothes after a single day — May's explicit instruction — rush past in a montage that erases the novel's sustained meditation on grief as both burden and sacrament.

The Daughters of Mary and Black Madonna Theology

August's Daughters of Mary — the congregation of Black women who meet in the Boatwrights' honey house to pray before a life-sized wooden statue of a black Virgin — embody Kidd's central argument that divinity can be female and Black and homemade. The novel details the statue's history: carved by August's grandfather Obadiah, who was a slave, she's named Our Lady of Chains because he found her as a ship's figurehead. August's storytelling about the statue's miracles takes up entire chapters, and the Assumption Day vigil — when the women dress Mary in a red cloak and carry her to the river — occupies a lush, ceremonial section of the book. The film includes the statue and shows one Daughters of Mary meeting, but Queen Latifah's August delivers the origin story as a single monologue while sitting on the porch, compressing mystery into information. The Assumption Day ceremony is reduced to a brief nighttime procession with candles and humming. Kidd's prose makes the rituals strange and specific: the women touch Mary's heart and pray in call-and-response, they smear honey on her as an offering, and Lily experiences a vision of bees covering her body that triggers her first real understanding of forgiveness. The film keeps the honey on the statue but cuts Lily's hallucinatory experience entirely, making her spiritual transformation a matter of watching other people pray rather than being undone by something inexplicable.

Lily and Zach's Romance Under Jim Crow

Zach Taylor, August's godson who wants to become a lawyer, and Lily conduct their romance in the novel with full awareness that being seen together could get Zach killed. Their first kiss happens in the honey house after weeks of stolen conversations, and Kidd writes the scene with the tension of contraband — Lily knows she's been taught to see Zach as forbidden, and the novel forces her to confront how white supremacy has colonized even her desire. When Zach is arrested after his friend throws a bottle at a white man, the book shows him coming back from jail changed, angrier, and he tells Lily they can't be together until the world changes enough to make it safe. The film, with Tristan Wilds playing Zach, includes the jailing but smooths the edges of his radicalization. The movie's Zach is noble and patient; the book's Zach tells Lily, "There's no place for us to be together right now," and later, "I'm going to make the world a place where you and I can be together." The film invents a closing title card claiming Zach became a lawyer and implying reunion; Kidd ends with Zach and Lily apart, writing letters, their future entirely uncertain. The novel refuses the comfort of a love-conquers-all resolution, insisting instead that Jim Crow is a material reality that doesn't bend for teenage longing.

T. Ray's Confrontation and Lily's Choice to Stay

When T. Ray tracks Lily to the Boatwright house in the novel's climax, he finds her in the honey house in the middle of the night and tries to drag her back to Sylvan. The scene is long and terrifying — T. Ray calls Lily "Deborah" by mistake, revealing that he's never stopped conflating his daughter with his dead wife, and in his confusion he nearly softens before rage takes over again. August intervenes, standing between T. Ray and Lily, and when he raises his hand to strike, the Daughters of Mary emerge from the shadows where they've been praying, forming a silent wall of Black women who refuse to let him pass. It's a scene of collective protection, and T. Ray leaves without Lily because he's outnumbered and because August's steady authority unmans him. Paul Bettany plays T. Ray's arrival in the film with the same fury, and the mistaken "Deborah" moment is intact, but the scene is shorter and the Daughters of Mary don't appear. August faces T. Ray alone, and he capitulates after she tells him Lily is staying, which makes his departure feel like a single woman's persuasion rather than a community's refusal to surrender a child to violence. The book's version insists that Lily's survival depends on a network of women, not a heroic individual; the film makes it simpler and less politically resonant.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first if you want to understand why Lily stays in Tiburon. The novel earns her transformation by showing how August's beekeeping lessons — about queen bees, about the hive's collective intelligence — become metaphors Lily uses to rebuild her understanding of motherhood, forgiveness, and belonging. The film shows the lessons but doesn't give them time to accumulate into meaning. When August tells Lily that her mother loved her, it's a revelation the book has prepared for across two hundred pages of Lily's self-interrogation; in the film it's a climactic speech that arrives without the same emotional architecture. If you watch first, you'll get a capable, well-acted distillation that resolves its conflicts neatly. You won't realize how much patience and strangeness the novel requires, or how much messier and more uncomfortable Lily's reckonings with race and matricide actually are.

The book also refuses the film's tidy ending. Kidd closes with Lily living in the pink house, attending a white high school where she's now the outcast, writing to Zach, and thinking about the difference between the mother who died and the mothers she's found. The film ends with voice-over about how people need love and Lily smiling in a golden-hour glow. If you want the story where a white girl's education in Black resilience costs her something and doesn't guarantee her a happy ending, you need the novel.

Verdict

The book wins because it makes Lily's guilt unbearable and her refuge in the Boatwright house a temporary mercy, not a permanent solution — the film lets her off easier and resolves what Kidd leaves raw. Okonedo's May is heartbreaking, but she can't rescue a script that trades the novel's spiritual strangeness for inspirational uplift.
