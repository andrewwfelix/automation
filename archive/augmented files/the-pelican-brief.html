The Story in Brief

Tulane law student Darby Shaw writes a speculative legal brief connecting the assassinations of Supreme Court justices Rosenberg and Jensen to a Louisiana oil magnate's plan to drill in a protected pelican breeding marsh. When her lover and constitutional law professor Thomas Callahan dies in a car explosion meant for her, Darby realizes her brief — passed through Callahan to his friend at the FBI — has reached the conspirators. She goes underground in New Orleans, eventually contacting Washington Herald investigative reporter Gray Grantham, who's been receiving cryptic calls from a source inside the conspiracy. Together they pursue the truth while hired assassin Khamel stalks Darby through the French Quarter.

Alan J. Pakula directed the 1993 adaptation with Julia Roberts as Darby and Denzel Washington as Grantham, banking on star power just as Grisham fever was peaking — The Firm had opened six months earlier to massive box office. Warner Bros. fast-tracked production to capitalize on Grisham's brand, resulting in a Christmas 1993 release that earned $195 million worldwide. The film works as slick, watchable entertainment and benefits from Pakula's experience with paranoid political thrillers like All the President's Men, but it sacrifices the novel's intricate plotting and sustained dread for Hollywood romance and a sanitized conspiracy that feels half as dangerous as the one Grisham wrote.

Key Differences

Darby's Terror and Isolation

In the novel, Darby spends fifty pages alone after Callahan's death — dyeing her hair in a Baton Rouge motel, burning her identification, sleeping in her car, moving every few hours. Grisham writes her paranoia in exhausting detail: she checks rearview mirrors obsessively, never makes calls from the same phone twice, keeps a .25 automatic in her purse that she nearly fires at a bellhop. When she finally reaches Grantham by phone, she's been underground for five days and sounds unhinged. Roberts plays Darby as startled but fundamentally composed, her fear registering as wide eyes rather than the bone-deep exhaustion Grisham describes. The film collapses her isolation to perhaps twenty-four hours. We see her cut and dye her hair in one brief motel scene, then she's in Washington meeting Grantham face-to-face at a museum — no week of paranoid solitude, no sense that she's been ground down by fear. Pakula opts for a brisk pace that keeps Roberts glamorous rather than haggard, but this robs Darby of the desperation that makes her survival feel earned rather than scripted.

Grantham's Professional Stakes

The novel gives Grantham a subplot the film eliminates entirely: his career is in jeopardy because his editor Smith Keen and the paper's lawyers doubt his anonymous source, a man who calls himself Garcia. Grantham spends chapters arguing with Keen, who threatens to kill the story unless Grantham produces Garcia or corroborating evidence. This professional pressure parallels Darby's physical danger — both are isolated, both are running out of time. Washington's Grantham faces no such skepticism. His editor, played by John Lithgow, is supportive from the first scene and never wavers. The film also cuts Grantham's bitter divorce and his fraying relationship with his source at the FBI, both of which Grisham uses to show a man whose personal life has been sacrificed to investigative work. Washington plays Grantham as smooth and confident, a reporter who dresses in expensive suits and never seems to doubt himself. He's appealing, but he's not the obsessive, slightly rumpled journalist living in newsroom fluorescence that Grisham wrote. Without professional or personal stakes, Grantham becomes a vehicle for exposition rather than a character with something to lose.

The Conspiracy's Scope and Specificity

Grisham's brief names Victor Mattiece, a reclusive oil billionaire who's spent eight years and $600 million trying to overturn environmental protections for a marsh in Terrebonne Parish where his company owns drilling rights. The novel details Mattiece's donations to President Coal's campaign, the Justice Department's stalled lawsuit, and the specific Supreme Court split — Rosenberg and Jensen were the swing votes blocking a rehearing that would have favored Mattiece. The book includes legal memos, describes lower court rulings, and explains why this particular patch of Louisiana wetland is worth killing for: $50 billion in recoverable oil. Pakula's film keeps Mattiece and the pelican marsh but glosses over the legal mechanism. We never learn which case is at stake or why two dead justices would change the outcome. The brief itself is treated as a McGuffin — characters say it's dangerous, but the film doesn't show the specific chain of judicial logic that makes it explosive. The conspiracy also feels smaller on screen. In the novel, Mattiece employs a network of lawyers, fixers, and contract killers across three states. The film reduces this to a handful of assassins and one scene in which Mattiece — played briefly by a voice on a speakerphone — barks orders. The result is a conspiracy that feels functional for the plot rather than chillingly plausible.

The Romance That Wasn't

Pakula's most consequential change is the romantic chemistry he engineers between Darby and Grantham. The film ends with the two of them in a beachside Caribbean resort, holding hands and gazing at the ocean — a couple who've survived danger and found love. Grisham wrote no such relationship. His Darby and Grantham are allies, nothing more. They meet exactly twice in person before the climax, speak mainly by phone, and maintain a wary professional distance. Darby is grieving Callahan, whose death happens on page 54, and she spends the rest of the novel numb and focused solely on survival. Grantham is married — unhappily, and headed for divorce, but still married. There's no flirtation, no longing glances, no romantic tension. The novel ends with Darby testifying before a grand jury and then disappearing to the Caribbean alone to recover, while Grantham stays in D.C. to write the story. By pairing Roberts and Washington and giving them a romantic arc, Pakula softens the novel's bleak emotional terrain — Darby's trauma becomes something a relationship can heal. Grisham suggests the opposite: the brief has cost her everything, and there's no comfort waiting at the end, only survival.

Khamel's Expertise and Exit

The assassin Khamel is a shadowy legend in the novel — a multilingual killer fluent in nine languages, a master of disguise who once impersonated a Venezuelan diplomat for six weeks. Grisham devotes a chapter to Khamel's methodology: he studies targets for days, learns their routines, kills them in ways that look like accidents or natural causes. His contract on Darby requires him to make her death look random, a mugging or rape gone wrong. Khamel tracks Darby through the French Quarter, follows her to a bookstore, nearly corners her in a hotel lobby. When Grantham finally identifies him using an FBI sketch artist and old Interpol files, the revelation feels earned — we've spent a hundred pages watching Khamel work. The film gives Khamel two scenes. He appears briefly at a Georgetown restaurant where he impersonates a State Department official to poison an FBI agent, then vanishes from the story. We never see him hunt Darby, never get a sense of his methods or reputation. His role as the relentless pursuer is transferred to generic hitmen in dark sedans. By eliminating Khamel's menace, the film loses the specific, personal danger that Grisham sustains across two hundred pages — the sense that Darby is being hunted not by thugs but by the world's most patient killer.

Should You Read First?

Read the book first, because Pakula's film will make you think the conspiracy is simpler and the danger less credible than it actually is. If you watch Roberts and Washington take a Caribbean vacation together after two hours of moderate peril, you'll have no sense of the week Darby spends alone and terrified, sleeping in her Honda, or the months Grantham spends battling his editor to keep the story alive. The novel earns its paranoia with specificity — the legal briefs, the oil rights, the Supreme Court vote tallies — that makes Mattiece's willingness to kill justices feel horrifyingly rational. The film asks you to accept the danger as a given and move on to the next scene.

If you read first, the movie becomes a competent but watered-down version that nevertheless benefits from Pakula's assured direction and two stars at the peak of their appeal. If you watch first, you'll find the novel slower and more procedural than expected, but also far more frightening — because Grisham shows you exactly how a legal brief can be a death sentence, and why no one is coming to save Darby Shaw.

Verdict

Grisham's novel delivers the sustained dread and legal intricacy that Pakula's film sands down for star-vehicle romance. The book makes you believe a law student's memo could get her killed; the movie makes you believe Julia Roberts and Denzel Washington look good running from actors in sedans.
