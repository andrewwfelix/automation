The Story in Brief

Everything, Everything follows Madeline Whittier, a teenager with a rare immunodeficiency disorder that forces her to live in a sterile, sealed-off environment. When a new family moves in next door, she meets and falls for the son, Olly. As their relationship deepens, Madeline must decide whether to risk her life for a chance at love and the outside world.

The book was a hit YA romance, praised for its exploration of themes like isolation, disability, and the power of human connection. The 2017 film adaptation, directed by Stella Meghie, attempted to capture the story's emotional core but fell short in several key areas.

Key Differences

Madeline's Characterization

In the book, Madeline is a complex, relatable protagonist. She feels powerless in her restricted life but also curious about the world beyond, and the narration gives insight into her inner thoughts and struggles. The film, however, presents a more one-dimensional Madeline, lacking the nuance and depth of the literary version. Amandla Stenberg's performance is solid, but the script doesn't allow her to fully inhabit the character's emotional arc.

Olly's Role

Olly, Madeline's love interest, is a more compelling and well-rounded character in the book. His backstory, family dynamics, and motivations are more fleshed out, making his relationship with Madeline feel more authentic. In the film, Nick Robinson's Olly is serviceable but lacks the same level of complexity and emotional investment.

Madeline's Illness

The book delves deeper into the specifics and implications of Madeline's rare condition, SCID (severe combined immunodeficiency). This adds a layer of realism and gravity to her situation that the film doesn't quite capture. The movie tends to gloss over the medical details, missing an opportunity to truly convey the challenges Madeline faces.

Ending and Twist

The book's climactic twist, which casts Madeline's entire story in a new light, is a powerful and impactful moment. The film attempts to replicate this reveal, but the execution feels rushed and lacks the emotional resonance of the literary version. Viewers who haven't read the book may find the ending somewhat underwhelming.

Tone and Pacing

The book strikes a careful balance between the weightiness of Madeline's situation and the blossoming romance, creating an intimate, character-driven experience. The film, on the other hand, feels overly polished and at times rushed, missing some of the subtlety and nuance of the source material.

Should You Read First?

If you're a fan of the YA romance genre and want to experience the full emotional impact of the story, we highly recommend reading the book first. Nicola Yoon's novel provides a richer, more immersive exploration of Madeline's journey, with more developed characters and a more satisfying resolution. While the film adaptation is an adequate attempt to bring the story to the screen, the book is the superior version and will likely leave a stronger impression.

That said, the movie can still be an enjoyable experience, especially for those who prefer a more visually-driven take on the narrative. Just temper your expectations and be prepared for some of the subtleties and emotional depth to be lost in translation.

Verdict

The book is the clear winner, with a more nuanced and impactful story that the film adaptation doesn't quite capture.
